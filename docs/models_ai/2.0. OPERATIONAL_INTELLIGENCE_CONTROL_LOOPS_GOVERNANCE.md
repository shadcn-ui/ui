## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document defines the **non-negotiable governance requirements** for operational intelligence and control loops within Ocean ERP. It establishes how the organization **detects, acts on, and self-corrects** operational deviations without requiring executive intervention.

**Phase 1 answered:** "What must executives decide today?"  
**Phase 2 answers:** "How does the organization act and self-correct?"

**Operational Intelligence exists to:**
- Detect problems before executives notice
- Route actions to accountable roles
- Close control loops through verification
- Escalate only when control mechanisms fail

**Core Principle:**  
The system enforces operational discipline by detecting deviations early, assigning corrective actions to the right owners, and verifying resolution—all while keeping executives informed by exception, not by habit.

This is not an automation catalog. This is not a feature list. This is a **system of control** that enables organizational maturity and operational scale.

## 1. OPERATIONAL INTELLIGENCE DEFINITION

### 1.1 What is Operational Intelligence?

Operational intelligence is a **continuous monitoring and response system** that:

1. **Monitors** business operations against expected behavior and tolerance thresholds
2. **Interprets** deviations as actionable signals requiring correction
3. **Routes** signals to accountable owners with authority to act
4. **Executes** corrective actions (automated or manual)
5. **Verifies** that corrections achieved the desired outcome
6. **Escalates** to higher authority only when control mechanisms fail

Operational intelligence is **not**:
- A reporting system (that is analytics)
- An alert notification tool (that is messaging)
- A task management system (that is workflow)

It is a **closed-loop control system** that maintains operational stability.

### 1.2 The Prevention Principle

**"Operational intelligence exists to PREVENT executive attention."**

Executives should intervene in operations **only when:**
- Normal control loops have failed
- Risk severity exceeds operational authority
- Strategic decisions are required

If executives are routinely handling operational issues, one of three failures has occurred:

1. **Detection failure:** Problems are not caught early
2. **Ownership failure:** Corrective actions are not assigned to the right roles
3. **Execution failure:** Actions are not being taken or verified

The goal is **executive intervention by exception**, not by habit.

### 1.3 Self-Correction as Operational Maturity

A mature operation is one that can **self-correct** within established governance boundaries:

- **Immature operations:** Every deviation escalates to management
- **Maturing operations:** Routine deviations are corrected by operational teams
- **Mature operations:** Deviations are detected, corrected, and verified without escalation

Operational intelligence accelerates organizational maturity by **institutionalizing control discipline**.

### 1.4 Relationship to Executive Layer

Operational intelligence (Phase 2.0) operates **below** the executive signal layer (Phase 1.4):

| Layer | Purpose | Audience | Intervention Model |
|-------|---------|----------|-------------------|
| **Executive (Phase 1.4)** | Strategic decisions, high-severity risks | CEO, CFO, COO | By exception only |
| **Operational (Phase 2.0)** | Tactical corrections, routine deviations | Operations teams, managers | Continuous, self-correcting |

**Data flow:**
- Operational control loops handle most deviations
- Unresolved or high-severity deviations escalate to executive layer
- Executives see only what operations could not resolve

This separation is non-negotiable.

## 2. SIGNAL → ALERT → ACTION → RESOLUTION LOOP

### 2.1 The Closed-Loop Control Model

Every operational control follows a **mandatory closed-loop lifecycle**:

```
┌─────────┐      ┌─────────┐      ┌─────────┐      ┌────────────┐
│ SIGNAL  │  →   │  ALERT  │  →   │ ACTION  │  →   │ RESOLUTION │
└─────────┘      └─────────┘      └─────────┘      └────────────┘
     ↑                                                      │
     └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                    (Verification & Closure)
```

**No signal may exist without a defined loop.**  
**No alert may exist without an owner.**  
**No action may close without verification.**

### 2.2 Loop Stage Definitions

#### Stage 1: SIGNAL (Detection)

A **signal** is a detected deviation from expected operational behavior.

Signal generation requires:
- **Baseline definition:** What is normal? (e.g., average daily orders = 1,200 ± 15%)
- **Deviation threshold:** What triggers action? (e.g., orders <1,020 or >1,380)
- **Business context:** Why does this matter? (e.g., affects daily revenue target)

Signal examples:
- Inventory for SKU-A dropped below safety stock (500 units, threshold 800)
- Fulfillment SLA breached for 15 orders (SLA: ship within 24 hours)
- Supplier payment overdue by 10 days (terms: Net 30)

**Signals are machine-generated, not human-reported.**

#### Stage 2: ALERT (Routing & Acknowledgment)

An **alert** is a routed notification requiring acknowledgment by an accountable owner.

Alert routing requires:
- **Owner assignment:** Who is responsible? (role, not person)
- **Severity classification:** How urgent? (Informational, Warning, Critical)
- **Action requirement:** What must be done? (clear instruction or decision)
- **Time sensitivity:** By when? (SLA for acknowledgment and action)

Alert example:
```
Alert ID: OPS-2026-00482
Type: Inventory Stockout Risk
Severity: Warning
Owner: Supply Chain Manager
Description: SKU-A inventory at 500 units (62% of safety stock).
             Projected stockout in 6 days if current demand continues.
Action Required: Review open POs or expedite supplier order.
Acknowledgment Due: Within 4 hours
Resolution Due: Within 24 hours
```

**Alerts require human acknowledgment.** Silence is treated as control failure.

#### Stage 3: ACTION (Execution)

An **action** is the corrective measure taken to resolve the deviation.

Action types:
- **Manual decision:** Human reviews data and takes action (approve PO, adjust price, contact supplier)
- **Semi-automated:** System recommends action, human approves (suggest reorder quantity, human confirms)
- **Fully automated:** System executes within governance boundaries (auto-reorder for low-risk items)

Action requirements:
- **Authority alignment:** Action must be within owner's Phase 1.1 authority
- **Audit trail:** Action logged per Phase 1.3 requirements
- **Reversibility:** If action can cause harm, it must be reversible or require approval

Action example:
- Supply Chain Manager reviews alert OPS-2026-00482
- Checks open PO #4521 (in-transit, arrives in 4 days)
- Updates alert: "PO in-transit, stockout risk mitigated"
- System monitors: If PO delays, re-alert

#### Stage 4: RESOLUTION (Verification & Closure)

**Resolution** is verified correction of the deviation and formal closure of the control loop.

Resolution requires:
- **Outcome verification:** Did the action resolve the signal? (inventory restocked, SLA recovered, payment processed)
- **Closure confirmation:** Owner confirms resolution (not auto-closed by system)
- **Loop audit:** Full signal → alert → action → resolution logged per Phase 1.3

Resolution outcomes:
- **Resolved:** Deviation corrected, signal cleared
- **Escalated:** Deviation persists, escalated to higher authority or executive layer
- **Accepted:** Deviation acknowledged but not corrected (requires justification and CFO/COO approval)

**No loop may auto-close without verification.**

### 2.3 Loop Integrity Rules

A control loop is **valid** only when:

1. **Signal is deterministic:** Same inputs produce same signal (reproducible)
2. **Alert has owner:** Every alert routes to exactly one accountable role
3. **Action has authority:** Owner has governance authority to act
4. **Resolution is verified:** Outcome is measured, not assumed

**Broken loops:**
- Signal without threshold → generates noise, not actionable alerts
- Alert without owner → no accountability
- Action without authority → governance bypass
- Resolution without verification → false closure

Broken loops are escalated to operational excellence teams for correction.

## 3. ALERT CLASSIFICATION & NOISE CONTROL

### 3.1 Alert Severity Tiers

Every alert must be classified into exactly one severity tier:

| Tier | Definition | Acknowledgment SLA | Resolution SLA | Escalation Path |
|------|------------|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Informational** | No immediate action required; awareness only | 24 hours | Not time-bound | None |
| **Warning** | Action required to prevent risk escalation | 4 hours | 24-48 hours | Operations Manager |
| **Critical** | Immediate action required; business continuity at risk | 1 hour | 4 hours | VP Operations, COO |

**Severity is assigned by system logic, not user preference.**

### 3.2 Alert Noise Control Rules

**Alert noise** (excessive, redundant, or low-value alerts) destroys operational discipline. The following rules are mandatory:

#### Rule 1: No Single-Outlier Alerts

Alerts must not trigger on **single outliers** without confirmation.

- **Prohibited:** Order volume drops to 400 once, alert fires
- **Required:** Order volume below 1,020 for 2+ hours, alert fires

Use **confirmation windows** to suppress transient spikes.

#### Rule 2: Duplicate Alert Grouping

If multiple instances of the same issue occur, they must be **grouped**, not repeated.

Example:
- 3 SKUs below safety stock → Single alert: "3 SKUs at stockout risk" (with drill-down)
- Not 3 separate alerts spamming the operations dashboard

#### Rule 3: Alert Storms Prohibited

If a single root cause generates >10 alerts within 1 hour, the system must:

1. **Suppress further alerts**
2. **Generate meta-alert:** "System detected alert storm: [root cause category]"
3. **Escalate to engineering team** to investigate detection logic

**Alert storms indicate system failure, not business failure.**

#### Rule 4: Repeated Alert Escalation

If the same alert fires repeatedly without resolution:

- **1st occurrence:** Warning-level alert
- **2nd occurrence (within 7 days):** Warning escalated to Critical
- **3rd occurrence:** Escalated to VP Operations / COO + root cause analysis required

**Repeated alerts indicate control failure, not normal operations.**

### 3.3 Alert Threshold Calibration

Alert thresholds must be **calibrated** to business tolerance:

- **Too sensitive:** Alert fires constantly, operations team ignores it (alert fatigue)
- **Too loose:** Alert fires too late, damage already done

**Calibration process:**
1. Define business tolerance (e.g., "We can tolerate 5% order variance")
2. Set initial threshold (e.g., alert if variance >7%)
3. Monitor false-positive rate (alerts that resolve without action)
4. Adjust threshold quarterly based on operational feedback

**Target:** <10% false-positive rate. If higher, threshold is too sensitive.

### 3.4 Prohibited Alert Patterns

The following alert patterns are **explicitly prohibited**:

- **Alerts without actions:** "Inventory is low" — OK, what should I do?
- **Alerts without thresholds:** "Orders changed" — changed by how much?
- **Alerts without owners:** Alert fires but no one is assigned
- **Alerts that cannot be acted upon:** "Supplier delayed shipment" (if we have no alternate supplier and cannot expedite)

If an alert pattern is detected as prohibited, it is automatically disabled and flagged for governance review.

## 4. CONTROL OWNERSHIP MODEL

### 4.1 The Single-Owner Principle

Every control loop must have **exactly ONE owner role**.

Ownership means:
- **Accountability** for resolution (not necessarily execution)
- **Authority** to take corrective action (per Phase 1.1)
- **Responsibility** to acknowledge alerts and verify outcomes

**Prohibited:**
- Shared ownership (two roles both responsible)
- No ownership (alert fires but no one assigned)
- Person-based ownership (alerts assigned to "John" instead of "Supply Chain Manager")

Ownership is **role-based**, never person-based.

### 4.2 Ownership Assignment Matrix

The following ownership assignments are **mandatory** for core operational domains:

| Control Domain | Alert Type | Owner Role | Escalation Path |
|----------------|------------|------------|-----------------|
| **Inventory** | Stockout risk | Supply Chain Manager | VP Operations |
| **Inventory** | Overstock exposure | Supply Chain Manager | CFO (financial impact) |
| **Fulfillment** | SLA breach | Operations Manager | VP Operations |
| **Fulfillment** | Backlog accumulation | Operations Manager | COO |
| **Procurement** | Supplier delay | Procurement Lead | Supply Chain Manager |
| **Procurement** | Price variance | Procurement Lead | CFO |
| **Finance Ops** | Payment overdue | Finance Operations Lead | CFO |
| **Finance Ops** | Cash flow risk | Finance Operations Lead | CFO (immediate) |
| **Governance** | Approval override | Compliance Manager | COO, CTO |
| **Governance** | Policy breach | Compliance Manager | CEO |

**Custom control loops** require governance review to assign ownership.

### 4.3 Delegation vs Accountability

Owners may **delegate execution** but cannot delegate **accountability**.

Example:
- **Owner:** Supply Chain Manager (accountable for resolution)
- **Delegate:** Junior Buyer (executes PO approval)
- **Accountability:** Supply Chain Manager verifies PO was approved and stockout prevented

If the delegate fails to act, the owner is accountable for the control failure.

### 4.4 Cross-Functional Ownership

Some control loops require **cross-functional coordination**:

Example: Cash flow risk
- **Primary owner:** Finance Operations Lead
- **Supporting roles:** AR Manager (expedite collections), AP Manager (delay payments if needed), CFO (strategic decisions)

**Primary owner** coordinates resolution but does not personally execute all actions.

### 4.5 Ownership Gaps as Governance Failure

If a control loop cannot assign an owner (no role has authority or visibility), it indicates:

1. **Authority gap:** Phase 1.1 governance does not define authority for this action
2. **Process gap:** No operational process exists to handle this deviation
3. **Data gap:** System cannot detect the deviation reliably

**System response:** Flag as governance gap, escalate to COO/CTO for resolution.

Do not create control loops without clear ownership.

## 5. AUTOMATION BOUNDARIES (CRITICAL)

### 5.1 Automation as Governed Execution

Automation within operational control loops is **permitted only within strict governance boundaries**.

**Automation is not autonomy.** Automation executes pre-approved logic within defined limits.

### 5.2 Automation Authorization Classes

All automated actions must be classified into one of three authorization classes:

| Class | Definition | Example | Governance Requirement |
|-------|------------|---------|------------------------|
| **Recommendation-Only** | System recommends action, human decides | Suggest reorder quantity for SKU | No approval required (zero risk) |
| **Auto-Action with Approval** | System executes after human approval | Auto-generate PO, requires manager approval | Phase 1.2 workflow approval required |
| **Auto-Action without Approval** | System executes immediately | Auto-allocate stock from available inventory | CFO/COO pre-authorization required |

**Class 3 (Auto-Action without Approval) is rare and high-risk.**

### 5.3 Prohibited Automation

The following actions **must never** be fully automated without human approval:

- **Financial transactions:** Payments, write-offs, refunds, journal entries
- **Inventory adjustments:** Stock corrections, scrap decisions, transfers between legal entities
- **HR decisions:** Hiring, termination, salary changes, performance ratings
- **Governance overrides:** Approval bypasses, policy exceptions, authority escalations
- **Master data changes:** Pricing, credit limits, supplier bank accounts, chart of accounts

**Rationale:** These actions have irreversible business or legal impact.

### 5.4 Reversibility Requirement

If an automated action is **irreversible** or **high-consequence**, it must require human approval.

Examples:
- **Reversible (can auto-execute):** Send email reminder to customer for overdue payment
- **Irreversible (requires approval):** Write off bad debt (affects financial statements)

**Test:** "If this action executes incorrectly, can we undo it without material business impact?"
- Yes → May be automated (with audit trail)
- No → Requires approval

### 5.5 Automation Audit Trail

Every automated action must generate a **Phase 1.3 audit event** including:

- What action was taken
- What trigger condition caused it
- What authority rule permitted automation (reference to governance policy)
- What outcome occurred
- How it can be reversed (if applicable)

**Automated actions without audit trails are prohibited.**

### 5.6 Automation Failure Behavior

When automation **fails** (logic error, data unavailable, external system down), the system must:

1. **Halt execution** (do not proceed with partial or assumed data)
2. **Generate alert** to owner for manual intervention
3. **Log failure** as critical operational event
4. **Preserve state** (do not corrupt data by partial execution)

**Fail-safe, not fail-forward.**

## 6. CONTROL LOOP PRIORITY DOMAINS

### 6.1 Mandatory Control Domains

The following operational domains **must** have active control loops. These are non-optional and must be operational before Phase 2.0 is considered production-ready.

---

#### A. INVENTORY CONTROL LOOPS

**Purpose:** Prevent stockouts and minimize excess inventory exposure.

##### Control Loop A1: Stockout Prevention

- **Signal:** Inventory on-hand drops below safety stock threshold
- **Alert:** "SKU-X at stockout risk: 6 days remaining at current demand"
- **Owner:** Supply Chain Manager
- **Action:** Review open POs, expedite supplier order, or source alternate supplier
- **Resolution:** Inventory restocked above safety stock, or justification accepted

##### Control Loop A2: Overstock Reduction

- **Signal:** Inventory aging >90 days and exceeds $50K valuation
- **Alert:** "SKU-Y overstock: $85K aging inventory, seasonal demand ended"
- **Owner:** Supply Chain Manager, escalate to CFO if write-off needed
- **Action:** Discount pricing, liquidation, or write-off approval
- **Resolution:** Inventory reduced to acceptable aging, or write-off executed

##### Control Loop A3: Allocation Enforcement

- **Signal:** Allocated inventory exceeds available inventory (oversell risk)
- **Alert:** "SKU-Z oversold: 1,200 units allocated but only 900 available"
- **Owner:** Operations Manager
- **Action:** Revise allocations, delay shipments, or expedite replenishment
- **Resolution:** Allocations match availability, no customer commitment breach

---

#### B. FULFILLMENT CONTROL LOOPS

**Purpose:** Maintain SLA compliance and prevent backlog accumulation.

##### Control Loop B1: SLA Breach Recovery

- **Signal:** Orders exceed SLA threshold (e.g., >24 hours from order to ship)
- **Alert:** "15 orders breached 24-hour ship SLA, average delay 36 hours"
- **Owner:** Operations Manager
- **Action:** Prioritize delayed orders, increase warehouse capacity, or notify customers
- **Resolution:** SLA breach rate returns to <5%

##### Control Loop B2: Backlog Balancing

- **Signal:** Unfulfilled order count exceeds 2x normal baseline
- **Alert:** "Order backlog at 1,200 (normal: 500), projected to delay 3+ days"
- **Owner:** Operations Manager, escalate to COO if capacity constraint
- **Action:** Add temporary labor, extend warehouse hours, or throttle inbound orders
- **Resolution:** Backlog reduced to normal levels

##### Control Loop B3: Capacity Throttling

- **Signal:** Warehouse operating at >90% capacity
- **Alert:** "Warehouse at 95% capacity, cannot absorb expected campaign surge"
- **Owner:** VP Operations
- **Action:** Pause marketing campaigns, divert orders to alternate warehouse, or delay low-priority shipments
- **Resolution:** Capacity utilization returns to <85%

---

#### C. PROCUREMENT CONTROL LOOPS

**Purpose:** Mitigate supplier risk and control procurement cost variance.

##### Control Loop C1: Supplier Delay Mitigation

- **Signal:** Supplier shipment delayed >7 days beyond expected delivery
- **Alert:** "Supplier ABC delayed PO #4521 by 10 days, stockout risk imminent"
- **Owner:** Procurement Lead
- **Action:** Escalate to supplier, source alternate, or expedite via air freight
- **Resolution:** Goods received or alternate source secured

##### Control Loop C2: Price Variance Detection

- **Signal:** PO unit price exceeds standard cost by >15%
- **Alert:** "PO #4522 unit price $12.50 vs standard $10.00 (+25%), margin impact $4,500"
- **Owner:** Procurement Lead, escalate to CFO if variance >$10K
- **Action:** Negotiate with supplier, reject PO, or approve variance with justification
- **Resolution:** Variance accepted or PO revised

##### Control Loop C3: Approval Acceleration

- **Signal:** PO pending approval >3 business days
- **Alert:** "5 POs awaiting approval >3 days, total value $125K"
- **Owner:** Procurement Lead (to chase approvers)
- **Action:** Reminder to approvers, escalate to manager, or revise PO to lower value
- **Resolution:** POs approved or escalated

---

#### D. FINANCIAL OPERATIONS LOOPS

**Purpose:** Protect cash flow and detect margin leakage.

##### Control Loop D1: Payment Follow-Up

- **Signal:** AR invoice overdue by >15 days
- **Alert:** "Customer XYZ invoice $45K overdue by 20 days, credit limit approaching"
- **Owner:** Finance Operations Lead
- **Action:** Contact customer, offer payment plan, or escalate to collections
- **Resolution:** Payment received or collections initiated

##### Control Loop D2: Cash Flow Protection

- **Signal:** Projected cash balance drops below $2M policy threshold within 14 days
- **Alert:** "Cash flow risk: Projected shortfall of $350K in 10 days"
- **Owner:** Finance Operations Lead, escalate to CFO immediately
- **Action:** Expedite AR collections, delay AP payments, or secure short-term credit
- **Resolution:** Cash balance remains above threshold

##### Control Loop D3: Margin Leakage Correction

- **Signal:** Gross margin for product category drops >5 percentage points
- **Alert:** "Electronics margin dropped from 38% to 32%, cause: unplanned discounts"
- **Owner:** Finance Operations Lead + Sales Manager (joint)
- **Action:** Review discount approvals, adjust pricing, or revise promotions
- **Resolution:** Margin returns to target or variance justified

---

#### E. GOVERNANCE ENFORCEMENT LOOPS

**Purpose:** Detect and correct governance erosion before audit failure.

##### Control Loop E1: Approval Bypass Detection

- **Signal:** Action executed without required approval (Phase 1.2 violation)
- **Alert:** "Payment $15K processed without CFO approval (threshold: $10K)"
- **Owner:** Compliance Manager
- **Action:** Investigate authorization, reverse if fraudulent, or request post-approval
- **Resolution:** Approval obtained retroactively or violation logged

##### Control Loop E2: Policy Violation Correction

- **Signal:** Segregation of duties (SoD) violation detected
- **Alert:** "User Jane created and approved PO #4530 (SoD violation)"
- **Owner:** Compliance Manager
- **Action:** Revoke approval, require secondary approval, or adjust role permissions
- **Resolution:** SoD violation corrected, no recurrence

##### Control Loop E3: Repeated Override Escalation

- **Signal:** Same user executes approval override >3 times in 30 days
- **Alert:** "User Bob overrode approval workflow 5 times this month"
- **Owner:** Compliance Manager, escalate to COO
- **Action:** Review justifications, revise authority limits, or investigate abuse
- **Resolution:** Override pattern addressed or user authority revised

---

### 6.2 Control Loop Prioritization

When multiple control loops fire simultaneously, prioritization is:

1. **Critical alerts** (business continuity risk)
2. **Governance violations** (legal/audit risk)
3. **Financial impact alerts** (cash flow, margin)
4. **Operational alerts** (SLA, inventory)
5. **Informational alerts** (awareness only)

Prioritization logic is fixed and cannot be user-customized.

## 7. TIME-BASED CONTROL ESCALATION

### 7.1 Control Loop Timeline

Every control loop operates on a **strict timeline** to prevent silent failures:

| Stage | Definition | Time Limit | Consequence of Delay |
|-------|------------|------------|----------------------|
| **T+0** | Alert generated | Immediate | N/A |
| **T+X** | Owner acknowledgment required | Varies by severity (1-24 hours) | Escalation to manager |
| **T+Y** | Action must be taken | Varies by severity (4-48 hours) | Escalation to VP/COO |
| **T+Z** | Resolution verified | Varies by severity (4-72 hours) | Escalation to executive layer (Phase 1.4) |

**Silence is treated as failure of control.**

### 7.2 Acknowledgment SLAs by Severity

| Severity | Acknowledgment SLA | Escalation if Missed |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Critical** | 1 hour | VP Operations |
| **Warning** | 4 hours | Operations Manager |
| **Informational** | 24 hours | No escalation (but logged) |

### 7.3 Resolution SLAs by Severity

| Severity | Resolution SLA | Escalation if Missed |
|----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Critical** | 4 hours | COO, escalate to Phase 1.4 Executive Dashboard |
| **Warning** | 24-48 hours | VP Operations |
| **Informational** | No time limit | N/A |

### 7.4 Escalation Path Requirements

Every control loop must define:

- **Primary owner:** First line of accountability
- **Escalation target:** Manager or VP if owner fails to acknowledge
- **Executive escalation:** COO/CFO if resolution SLA is missed

Escalation is **automatic**, not manual.

### 7.5 Escalation Audit Trail

Every escalation generates an audit event (Phase 1.3) recording:

- Alert ID and original owner
- Reason for escalation (acknowledgment missed, resolution missed)
- Escalation target and timestamp
- Outcome (resolved by escalation target, or further escalated)

Escalation patterns are reviewed monthly by operational excellence teams.

## 8. OPERATIONAL VS EXECUTIVE VISIBILITY

### 8.1 The Visibility Separation Principle

**Operational dashboards and executive dashboards must be separate.**

| Dashboard Type | Purpose | Audience | Content |
|----------------|---------|----------|---------|
| **Operational Dashboard** | Task execution, queue management, alert resolution | Operations teams, managers | All alerts, all control loops, task queues |
| **Executive Dashboard (Phase 1.4)** | Strategic decisions, unresolved high-severity risks | CEO, CFO, COO | Only escalated or unresolved critical risks |

**This separation is non-negotiable.**

### 8.2 What Operations Teams See

Operational dashboards show:

- **All active alerts** (Informational, Warning, Critical)
- **Alert queues** organized by owner role
- **Task assignments** with SLA countdowns
- **Resolution history** (for learning and pattern detection)
- **Control effectiveness metrics** (MTTD, MTTA, MTTR)

Operations teams work in **task mode**: acknowledge, act, verify, close.

### 8.3 What Executives See

Executive dashboards (Phase 1.4) show:

- **Unresolved critical risks** that exceeded operational control
- **Governance violations** that require strategic intervention
- **Control failures** (repeated escalations, unresolved loops)
- **Business health indicators** (not raw operational tasks)

Executives work in **decision mode**: intervene only when operations cannot self-correct.

### 8.4 The Escalation Filter

Not all operational alerts escalate to executives. The **escalation filter** is:

✅ **Escalates to executives:**
- Critical alert unresolved after SLA (operational control failed)
- Governance violation requiring strategic decision
- Pattern of repeated failures (systemic issue)
- Financial impact exceeds operational authority (per Phase 1.1)

❌ **Does not escalate to executives:**
- Warning-level alerts acknowledged and in progress
- Resolved alerts (even if initially critical)
- Informational alerts
- Routine operational tasks

**Executives never see raw operational noise.**

### 8.5 No Hiding Unresolved Risks

Operations teams **must not hide unresolved risks** from executives.

Prohibited behaviors:
- Marking alerts as "resolved" without verification
- Suppressing alerts to avoid escalation
- Manually editing metrics to show better performance
- Delaying alert generation to avoid SLA breach detection

**All control loop data flows to Phase 1.3 audit logs.** Hiding risks is detectable and treated as governance violation.

## 9. CONTROL EFFECTIVENESS METRICS

### 9.1 Purpose of Control Metrics

Control effectiveness metrics measure **how well the control system operates**, not business outcomes.

**These are not business KPIs.**

| Metric Type | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| **Business KPIs** | Measure business outcomes (revenue, profit, customer satisfaction) |
| **Control Metrics** | Measure control system performance (detection speed, resolution speed, recurrence) |

Control metrics answer: "Is our operational intelligence system working?"

### 9.2 Mandatory Control Metrics

The following control metrics are **mandatory** and must be tracked for all control loops:

#### Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)

**Definition:** Average time from when a deviation occurs to when the system generates an alert.

**Target:** <15 minutes for critical deviations, <1 hour for warnings

**Purpose:** Measures how quickly the system detects problems

#### Mean Time to Acknowledge (MTTA)

**Definition:** Average time from alert generation to owner acknowledgment.

**Target:**
- Critical: <1 hour
- Warning: <4 hours

**Purpose:** Measures owner responsiveness

#### Mean Time to Resolve (MTTR)

**Definition:** Average time from alert generation to verified resolution.

**Target:**
- Critical: <4 hours
- Warning: <48 hours

**Purpose:** Measures control loop closure speed

#### Recurrence Rate

**Definition:** Percentage of resolved alerts that reoccur within 30 days.

**Target:** <10%

**Purpose:** Measures whether root causes are being addressed (not just symptoms)

### 9.3 Alert Quality Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **False Positive Rate** | Percentage of alerts that auto-resolve without action | <10% |
| **Escalation Rate** | Percentage of alerts that escalate beyond primary owner | <15% |
| **Acknowledgment Compliance** | Percentage of alerts acknowledged within SLA | >95% |
| **Resolution Compliance** | Percentage of alerts resolved within SLA | >90% |

### 9.4 Control Loop Health Dashboard

Operations excellence teams maintain a **control loop health dashboard** (not executive-facing) showing:

- MTTD, MTTA, MTTR trends over time
- Recurrence rate by control loop type
- Alert volume trends (detecting alert storms)
- Owner acknowledgment compliance by role
- Escalation patterns (which loops fail most often)

This dashboard is reviewed monthly to improve control system effectiveness.

### 9.5 No Vanity Metrics

The following metrics are **prohibited** from control effectiveness reporting:

- Total alerts generated (volume without context)
- Total tasks completed (without resolution verification)
- Percentage of automation (automation without control is not success)
- User satisfaction scores (subjective, not measurable)

Control metrics must be **objective, measurable, and actionable**.

## 10. FAILURE MODES & SAFE DEGRADATION

### 10.1 Control System Failure Scenarios

Operational control systems must define behavior when **failures occur**:

#### Failure Mode 1: Automation Failure

**Scenario:** Automated action fails (logic error, data unavailable, external system down)

**System Response:**
1. **Halt automation** (do not proceed with partial execution)
2. **Generate alert** to owner for manual intervention
3. **Log failure** as critical system event (Phase 1.3)
4. **Preserve state** (no data corruption)
5. **Escalate to engineering** if failure recurs >3 times

**Default:** Fail safe, not fail forward.

#### Failure Mode 2: Data Confidence Degraded

**Scenario:** Audit confidence <100% (incomplete data, reconciliation pending)

**System Response:**
1. **Mark affected control loops as "UNTRUSTED"**
2. **Suppress automated actions** dependent on degraded data
3. **Generate meta-alert:** "Control loop degraded: awaiting data reconciliation"
4. **Require manual review** for any actions in degraded loops
5. **Restore confidence** before resuming automation

**Default:** Manual control until trust is restored.

#### Failure Mode 3: Control Owner Unavailable

**Scenario:** Alert owner is on leave, resigned, or unresponsive

**System Response:**
1. **Route alert to backup owner** (manager or peer)
2. **Escalate immediately** (do not wait for primary owner SLA)
3. **Log exception** as control assignment gap
4. **Review ownership matrix** to prevent future gaps

**Default:** Escalate, do not block.

#### Failure Mode 4: Multiple Controls Conflict

**Scenario:** Two control loops recommend contradictory actions

Example:
- Control Loop A: "Increase inventory to prevent stockout"
- Control Loop B: "Reduce inventory to minimize overstock exposure"

**System Response:**
1. **Flag conflict** as control logic error
2. **Escalate to both owners** for joint decision
3. **Prevent automated execution** in either direction
4. **Require manual resolution** and governance review
5. **Revise control logic** to prevent future conflicts

**Default:** Human decision required.

### 10.2 Safe Degradation Principles

When control systems fail, the system must **degrade safely**:

| Principle | Behavior |
|-----------|----------|
| **Fail Safe** | Stop execution rather than proceed with uncertain data |
| **Manual Fallback** | Route to human decision when automation fails |
| **Preserve Audit** | Log all failures and manual interventions (Phase 1.3) |
| **Escalate Transparently** | Surface failures to operations and executive layers |

**Never suppress failure signals to maintain appearance of control.**

### 10.3 Recovery from Failure

Recovery process after control system failure:

1. **Root cause analysis:** What caused the failure?
2. **Validation:** Is the control system now operating correctly?
3. **Backfill:** Are there unresolved alerts missed during failure?
4. **Prevention:** What governance changes prevent recurrence?

Recovery requires CTO/COO sign-off before control loops resume automation.

## 11. AUDIT & TRACEABILITY INTEGRATION

### 11.1 Control Loop Audit Requirements

Every control loop must generate **Phase 1.3 audit events** at each stage:

| Stage | Audit Event | Required Attributes |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Signal Generation** | Deviation detected | Trigger condition, threshold crossed, baseline value, actual value |
| **Alert Routing** | Alert assigned to owner | Owner role, severity, time sensitivity, SLA deadlines |
| **Acknowledgment** | Owner acknowledges | Owner identity, timestamp, SLA compliance (met/missed) |
| **Action Execution** | Corrective action taken | Action type, authority reference, execution timestamp |
| **Resolution** | Outcome verified | Resolution outcome, verification method, loop closure timestamp |
| **Escalation** | Alert escalated | Reason (SLA missed), escalation target, timestamp |

**Control loop history is permanent and reviewable.**

### 11.2 Control Loop Immutability

Once a control loop is closed (resolved or escalated), it becomes **immutable**:

- Loop stages cannot be edited
- Timestamps cannot be altered
- Acknowledgments cannot be deleted

**If a loop was closed incorrectly, a new loop must be opened** with reference to the original.

### 11.3 Control Pattern Analysis

Audit data enables **pattern analysis** to detect systemic issues:

- **Recurring alerts:** Same signal fires repeatedly (root cause not addressed)
- **Escalation patterns:** Specific owner consistently misses SLAs
- **Control effectiveness trends:** MTTR increasing over time (control degradation)
- **Automation failures:** Specific automated actions fail repeatedly

Pattern analysis is conducted monthly by operational excellence teams.

### 11.4 Regulatory Alignment

Control loop audit trails support:

- **SOX-like internal controls:** Approval trails, segregation of duties enforcement
- **Financial audit:** Transaction-level traceability for material adjustments
- **Operational audit:** Process compliance verification

Auditors have read-only access to all control loop history.

## 12. CULTURAL ENFORCEMENT PRINCIPLE

### 12.1 Discipline, Not Surveillance

**"This system enforces discipline, not surveillance."**

The goal of operational intelligence is **not** to:
- Micromanage operations teams
- Punish individuals for missed SLAs
- Create fear or blame culture

The goal **is** to:
- Institutionalize operational discipline
- Detect problems early
- Enable self-correction
- Improve organizational maturity

### 12.2 The Operational Maturity Goal

A mature operation is characterized by:

- **Fewer fires:** Problems detected before they escalate
- **Faster correction:** Control loops close quickly
- **Less executive interruption:** Operations self-correct within governance boundaries
- **Higher trust:** Executives trust operations to handle routine deviations

Operational intelligence accelerates this maturity.

### 12.3 Learning Culture

Control loop data should be used for **learning, not punishment**:

- **Retrospectives:** What caused this alert? How do we prevent recurrence?
- **Threshold tuning:** Was this alert actionable? Should we adjust sensitivity?
- **Process improvement:** Do we need to revise governance rules or authority limits?

**If teams hide problems to avoid alerts, the system has failed.**

### 12.4 Transparency Principle

All control loop logic, thresholds, and ownership assignments must be **transparent**:

- Operations teams know what is being monitored
- Owners know what they are accountable for
- Thresholds are documented and justified
- Escalation paths are clear

**No hidden rules. No secret metrics.**

### 12.5 Executive Support Requirement

Operational intelligence requires **executive sponsorship**:

- COO ensures control loops are resourced and supported
- CFO ensures financial controls are enforced
- CTO ensures system reliability and audit integrity

**Without executive support, operational intelligence becomes "just another tool" that teams ignore.**

## CLOSING STATEMENT

Operational intelligence is the difference between **reactive firefighting and proactive control**.

Organizations that cannot detect deviations early will:
- Overload executives with operational noise
- Miss critical problems until they escalate
- Burn out operations teams with constant crises
- Fail to scale operations without proportional headcount

Organizations that institutionalize operational control loops will:
- Detect problems before customers notice
- Correct deviations before they require executive intervention
- Free executives to focus on strategic decisions
- Scale operations through discipline, not just headcount

**The choice is not whether to build operational intelligence.**

**The choice is whether to build it deliberately or suffer its absence.**

**Operations that cannot self-correct will eventually collapse under scale.**
