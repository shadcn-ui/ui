DOCUMENT PURPOSE & BINDING AUTHORITY
This document establishes the immutable boundaries and operational limits of the Ocean ERP workflow automation engine. It defines what workflows are permitted to control, what they are absolutely prohibited from controlling, and how workflow automation relates to governance authority established in Phase 1.1.

Critical Distinction: Governance defines authority. Workflow defines process. This document ensures workflow can never usurp, bypass, or redefine governance authority.

Deviation from this framework requires written approval from CTO, CFO, and COO with documented risk assessment, legal review, and audit committee notification.

This is not a feature specification or technical design. This is the operational boundary contract that prevents automation from becoming ungoverned execution.

SECTION 1: WORKFLOW ENGINE PURPOSE
1.1 Foundational Definition
The workflow engine is an execution mechanism, not a decision-making authority.

The workflow engine exists solely to:

Enforce governance decisions already defined in Phase 1.1
Route requests to authorized approvers based on predefined rules
Track approval chains for audit and compliance
Escalate stalled decisions according to governance timeframes
Surface exceptions requiring human judgment
Standardize repeatable approval processes
Reduce operational friction in compliant decision-making
The workflow engine does NOT:

Create new authority levels or permissions
Grant capabilities users lack through governance
Substitute for executive judgment on material matters
Override governance thresholds or immutability rules
Conceal accountability through automation
Bypass segregation of duties requirements
Replace human decision-making for non-routine matters
1.2 Governance vs. Workflow Relationship
Governance (Phase 1.1) defines WHO can decide.
Governance establishes roles, authority levels, thresholds, and approval requirements.

Workflow (Phase 1.2) defines HOW the decision is executed.
Workflow implements the routing, tracking, and enforcement of governance-defined authority.

Critical Principle: Workflow cannot change WHO has authority. It can only ensure the right WHO is consulted.

Example:

Governance states: "Purchase orders over $50K require Director approval"
Workflow implements: Route PO over $50K to user with Director role
Workflow CANNOT: Lower threshold to $100K
Workflow CANNOT: Allow Manager to approve if Director unavailable
Workflow CANNOT: Auto-approve if Director doesn't respond in 24 hours
1.3 Workflow as Governance Enforcement
The workflow engine is a governance enforcement tool, not an operational convenience feature.

Enforcement Responsibilities:

Prevent actions from proceeding without required authority
Route requests through proper approval chains
Enforce timeout and escalation rules
Capture audit evidence at each decision point
Prevent self-approval and segregation of duties violations
Surface policy exceptions for explicit override handling
Non-Enforcement (Prohibited):

Workflow cannot "soften" governance rules for convenience
Workflow cannot provide "emergency bypass" outside governance framework
Workflow cannot implement "fast-track approvals" that skip authority validation
Workflow cannot auto-approve based on historical patterns or trust
SECTION 2: GOVERNANCE PRIMACY RULE
2.1 Absolute Subordination
Rule 2.1.1: The workflow engine is absolutely subordinate to Phase 1.1 Governance Framework. In any conflict between workflow configuration and governance rules, governance rules prevail and workflow configuration is invalid.

Rule 2.1.2: Workflow configuration is an operational implementation of governance policy. Configuration changes cannot alter governance authority, thresholds, or immutability rules.

Rule 2.1.3: Workflow administrators configure processes. They do not configure authority. Authority configuration requires CTO + CFO approval per Phase 1.1.

2.2 Authority Source of Truth
Rule 2.2.1: Authority levels and thresholds defined in Phase 1.1 Section 5 (Decision Threshold Model) are the sole source of truth. Workflow engine reads these rules; it does not define them.

Rule 2.2.2: If workflow configuration references an authority level (e.g., "Manager approval"), the workflow must query the governance authority system in real-time. Workflow cannot cache or store authority assignments locally.

Rule 2.2.3: If a user's authority level changes, all pending workflows involving that user immediately re-evaluate authority requirements. Workflows do not complete based on stale authority data.

2.3 Configuration ≠ Permission
Rule 2.3.1: The ability to configure a workflow does not grant permission to bypass governance.

Example Violation:
Workflow administrator creates "expedited PO approval" workflow that routes $75K PO to Manager instead of required Director. This configuration is invalid regardless of business justification.

Rule 2.3.2: Workflow configuration changes that affect governed actions require governance review:

Route changes: Ensure proper authority levels
Timeout changes: Cannot violate Phase 1.1 escalation rules
Approval sequence changes: Cannot bypass segregation of duties
Conditional logic changes: Cannot create authority loopholes
Rule 2.3.3: Pre-defined workflow templates must be governance-certified before deployment. Custom workflows require case-by-case governance validation.

2.4 Governance Audit of Workflows
Rule 2.4.1: All workflow configurations are subject to quarterly governance audit:

Authority mappings verified against Phase 1.1 thresholds
Approval chains validated for segregation of duties
Timeout settings confirmed within escalation rules
Override paths examined for compliance
Rule 2.4.2: Workflow configurations found non-compliant are immediately suspended until remediated.

Rule 2.4.3: Repeated non-compliance by workflow administrator results in credential revocation.

SECTION 3: WORKFLOW-ELIGIBLE ACTIONS
The following action categories MAY be automated through workflow engine. Workflow suitability does not waive governance requirements.

3.1 Approval Request Routing
Eligible: Routing approval requests to appropriate authority levels based on governance rules.

Requirements:

Authority level determined by Phase 1.1 thresholds
Routing logic transparent and auditable
Requestor cannot manipulate routing
Backup approvers defined for unavailability
Example: Purchase order approval request automatically routed to Director for orders $50K-$200K, COO for orders >$200K.

3.2 Review Queues & Task Assignment
Eligible: Organizing pending items into review queues for authorized personnel.

Requirements:

Queue visibility restricted to authorized roles
Queue assignment based on governance authority
Queue metrics (age, priority) visible to supervisors
Stale queue items auto-escalate per Phase 1.1 Section 7
Example: Expense claims over $500 appear in Finance Manager queue. Claims over $5K appear in Finance Director queue.

3.3 Conditional Routing
Eligible: Routing requests through different paths based on attributes (amount, type, risk score).

Requirements:

Routing conditions based on objective criteria (amounts, categories, flags)
Conditions documented and auditable
Conditions cannot circumvent authority requirements
All paths lead to governance-compliant approvers
Example: Inventory adjustments due to damage route to Operations Manager. Adjustments due to theft route to Operations Director + Security.

3.4 Escalation Handling
Eligible: Automatic escalation when approvals exceed time limits defined in Phase 1.1 Section 7.

Requirements:

Escalation timeframes match Phase 1.1 specifications
Escalation to next authority level, not peer
Original approver notified of escalation
Escalation logged as governance event
Executive dashboard updated in real-time
Example: PO pending Manager approval for >24 hours escalates to Director automatically.

3.5 Timeout Enforcement
Eligible: Executing timeout actions defined in Phase 1.1 (auto-reject, auto-escalate, default decision).

Requirements:

Timeout duration from Phase 1.1 Section 7
Timeout action explicit (approve/reject/escalate)
Timeout triggering logged as governance event
Executive notification for material timeout actions
No silent timeouts
Example: Expense claim under $500 not approved in 72 hours: auto-approve. Over $500: escalate to Director.

3.6 Evidence Capture
Eligible: Capturing justifications, comments, attachments, and decision rationale.

Requirements:

Minimum evidence requirements defined per action type
Evidence immutable after submission
Evidence linked to audit trail
Insufficient evidence prevents workflow progression
Example: Discount approval request requires: customer name, reason, competitive pressure evidence, margin impact calculation.

3.7 Parallel Approvals
Eligible: Requiring approvals from multiple parties (e.g., Finance + Operations) before proceeding.

Requirements:

All required approvals must complete
Rejection by any party rejects entire request
Approval order enforced where sequence matters
No partial execution
Example: Supplier bank account change requires: Procurement Director approval + Finance Director approval + email confirmation from supplier.

3.8 Serial Approvals
Eligible: Requiring approvals in specific sequence (e.g., Manager → Director → CFO).

Requirements:

Sequence based on authority escalation
Cannot skip levels
Earlier rejection stops progression
Each step logged independently
Example: Journal entry $100K: Manager reviews → Director approves → CFO final approval.

3.9 Delegation Handling
Eligible: Routing to designated delegate when primary approver is unavailable.

Requirements:

Delegation must be pre-configured (not ad-hoc)
Delegate must have equal or greater authority
Delegation time-limited
Delegation logged and auditable
Executive approvals cannot be delegated (CFO, COO, CEO)
Example: Manager on vacation delegates approvals to peer Manager. Does not delegate to Supervisor.

SECTION 4: NON-WORKFLOWABLE ACTIONS (ABSOLUTE BOUNDARY)
The following actions must NEVER rely solely on workflow automation. These actions require explicit governance authorization at execution time, even if workflow approval has been completed.

4.1 Financial Posting & Modification
Prohibited: Workflow cannot trigger automatic posting of journal entries.

Rule: Journal entry approval via workflow authorizes the entry. Actual posting requires separate explicit action by authorized user with accounting role verification.

Rationale: Posting creates immutable financial record. This action requires conscious human execution with immediate accountability, not automated trigger.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve the journal entry for posting. Separate posting action validates authority at execution time.

4.2 Inventory Balance Manipulation
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically adjust inventory quantities.

Rule: Inventory adjustment approval via workflow authorizes the adjustment. Actual inventory change requires separate execution by operations personnel with real-time stock verification.

Rationale: Inventory adjustments affect financial valuation (COGS, asset values). Requires physical verification and conscious execution, not automated trigger.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve the adjustment request. Separate posting action validates current stock level and executes change.

4.3 Payment Execution
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically execute payments (wire transfers, checks, ACH).

Rule: Payment approval via workflow authorizes the payment. Actual fund transfer requires separate explicit action by treasury function with bank authentication.

Rationale: Payment execution is irreversible and fraud-risk. Requires final human verification of amount, recipient, and authority before funds move.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve the payment for execution. Treasury initiates actual payment with separate bank authentication.

4.4 Salary & Payroll Execution
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically process payroll or execute salary changes.

Rule: Salary change approval via workflow authorizes the change. Actual payroll processing requires explicit execution by HRIS with calculation verification.

Rationale: Payroll errors create legal liability and employee trust issues. Requires human verification of calculations, deductions, and compliance before execution.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve salary changes and payroll exceptions. Payroll processor executes with final verification step.

4.5 User Permission & Role Changes
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically grant user permissions or change roles.

Rule: Permission change approval via workflow authorizes the change. Actual permission modification requires separate action by system administrator with identity verification.

Rationale: Permission changes affect security posture. Requires verification of identity, authority, and business need before granting access.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve access requests. System admin executes change with final verification.

4.6 Period Closing & Locking
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically close or lock fiscal periods.

Rule: Period close approval via workflow authorizes the close. Actual period lock requires explicit action by CFO or Controller with completeness verification.

Rationale: Period close is irreversible (without CFO unlock). Requires verification that all period transactions are complete and accurate before locking.

Workflow Role: Workflow can manage period close checklist. CFO executes final lock.

4.7 Master Data Deletion
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically delete master data (customers, suppliers, products, employees).

Rule: Deletion approval via workflow authorizes deactivation. Actual deletion (if permitted) requires separate action with dependency verification.

Rationale: Master data deletion can break referential integrity and lose business history. Requires verification that no active dependencies exist.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve deactivation. System admin verifies no dependencies before deletion.

4.8 System Configuration Changes
Prohibited: Workflow cannot automatically modify system configurations (governance thresholds, business rules, integration settings).

Rule: Configuration change approval via workflow authorizes the change. Actual configuration modification requires separate action by system administrator with impact assessment.

Rationale: Configuration changes affect all users and can introduce governance violations. Requires final verification of impact and rollback plan.

Workflow Role: Workflow can approve change requests. System admin executes with testing and validation.

4.9 Audit Log Operations
Prohibited: Workflow cannot access, modify, export, or archive audit logs.

Rule: Audit log operations are governance-controlled actions outside workflow scope. Require compliance officer or external auditor authority.

Rationale: Audit logs must remain independent of operational workflows to preserve integrity. Workflow subjects (users, approvals) cannot control workflow evidence.

Workflow Role: None. Audit logs record workflow actions; workflows do not touch audit logs.

SECTION 6: APPROVAL ≠ EXECUTION RULE
This is the fundamental principle separating workflow authorization from action execution.

6.1 Approval Definition
Approval: A workflow outcome indicating that appropriate authority has authorized an action to proceed. Approval grants permission; it does not execute the action.

Approval Scope:

Verifies requestor has valid business need
Confirms action complies with policy
Validates authority level is sufficient
Captures justification and evidence
Creates audit record of authorization
Approval Does NOT:

Execute the approved action
Guarantee execution will occur
Bypass execution-time validation
Create irreversible state changes
6.2 Execution Definition
Execution: The actual performance of the approved action, which must occur as a separate, controlled step after workflow approval completes.

Execution Requirements:

Separate user action (not automatic trigger)
Real-time authority validation (even if approved)
Current state verification (conditions haven't changed)
Segregation of duties enforcement (executor ≠ approver)
Execution logging independent of approval logging
Execution subject to immutability rules (Phase 1.1 Section 8)
6.3 Two-Phase Commitment Model
Phase 1: Approval (Workflow-Controlled)

Request initiated by authorized user
Routed through approval chain per governance rules
Evidence and justification captured
Authority verification performed
Approval granted and logged
Approval Result: Action is AUTHORIZED but NOT EXECUTED

Phase 2: Execution (Governance-Controlled)

Authorized user (with execution role) initiates execution
System validates approval exists and is current
System validates current state (amounts, balances, dependencies)
System validates executor has execution authority
System validates segregation of duties (executor ≠ approver for material actions)
Execution performed and logged
Immutability rules applied if applicable
Execution Result: Action is COMPLETED and audit trail is sealed

6.4 Time Gap Handling
Rule 6.4.1: Approvals have expiration timeframes based on action materiality:

Low materiality (<$5K): Approval valid 30 days
Medium materiality ($5K-$50K): Approval valid 14 days
High materiality (>$50K): Approval valid 7 days
Rule 6.4.2: Expired approvals require re-approval. Cannot execute based on stale approval.

Rule 6.4.3: If material conditions change between approval and execution (e.g., inventory level, vendor status, budget availability), execution must re-validate approval validity.

6.5 Examples of Approval ≠ Execution
Example 6.5.1: Journal Entry

Approval: Director approves journal entry for $30K equipment depreciation
Execution: Accountant with GL posting authority posts the entry to ledger
Separation Reason: Posting creates immutable financial record; requires separate conscious action
Example 6.5.2: Purchase Order

Approval: Manager approves PO for $15K office supplies
Execution: Procurement administrator issues PO to supplier
Separation Reason: PO issuance creates legal commitment; requires verification of supplier status and budget availability at execution time
Example 6.5.3: Inventory Adjustment

Approval: Operations Director approves $8K inventory write-off for damaged goods
Execution: Warehouse supervisor posts inventory movement with photo documentation
Separation Reason: Inventory change affects financial valuation; requires physical verification before execution
Example 6.5.4: Salary Increase

Approval: CHRO approves 8% salary increase for employee
Execution: Payroll administrator updates employee record effective next payroll period
Separation Reason: Salary change affects recurring costs; requires verification of budget impact and payroll calculation before execution
6.6 Auto-Execution Prohibition for Governed Actions
Rule 6.6.1: Workflow approval completion MUST NOT trigger automatic execution of actions governed under Phase 1.1 Sections 3.1-3.6.

Rule 6.6.2: Workflow systems may display "Approved - Awaiting Execution" status but cannot initiate execution without separate human action.

Rule 6.6.3: Execution queues organize approved items awaiting execution, but execution remains manual and verified.

Rule 6.6.4: The only exception is non-material administrative actions (e.g., email notifications, status updates, report generation) which may auto-execute after approval.

SECTION 7: ESCALATION & TIMEOUT BEHAVIOR
Workflow escalation enforces the time-based governance rules defined in Phase 1.1 Section 7.

7.1 Mandatory Escalation Triggers
Trigger 7.1.1: Approval Timeout
When approval request exceeds time limit defined in Phase 1.1 Section 7.1, workflow automatically escalates to next authority level.

Escalation Actions:

Route request to higher authority
Notify original approver of escalation
Notify requestor of status change
Log escalation event in audit trail
Update executive dashboard in real-time
Trigger 7.1.2: Cumulative Value Threshold
When user exceeds cumulative transaction threshold (Phase 1.1 Section 7.2), all subsequent transactions auto-escalate regardless of individual amount.

Trigger 7.1.3: Pattern-Based Escalation
When system detects concerning patterns (Phase 1.1 Section 7.3), affected workflows auto-escalate for enhanced scrutiny:

Repeated rejections by same approver
Repeated override requests by same user
High approval velocity (potential rubber-stamping)
After-hours activity for material transactions
Trigger 7.1.4: Geographic Anomaly
When workflow request originates from unexpected location (IP address, VPN), request auto-escalates for security review.

7.2 Silence Equals Escalation, Not Approval
Rule 7.2.1: Lack of response within timeout period NEVER implies approval. System automatically escalates to next level.

Rule 7.2.2: No workflow may implement "deemed approved if not rejected within X hours" logic for governed actions.

Rule 7.2.3: Only non-material administrative requests (e.g., conference room booking, equipment requests under $500) may use timeout approval logic, and only with explicit executive permission.

7.3 Escalation Chains
Rule 7.3.1: Escalation follows authority hierarchy defined in Phase 1.1 Section 4.1:

Operator → Supervisor → Manager → Director → CFO/COO → CEO
Rule 7.3.2: Escalation cannot skip levels. If Director is unavailable, escalates to CFO/COO, not back to Manager.

Rule 7.3.3: Executive-level approvals (CFO, COO, CEO) cannot escalate further. Timeout at executive level triggers default action (typically reject) with board notification for material items.

Rule 7.3.4: Escalation includes full context:

Original request details
Why escalated (timeout, threshold breach, pattern)
Previous approval attempts and rejections
Business impact of delay
Recommended action
7.4 Escalation Notification Requirements
Rule 7.4.1: Real-Time Notifications
Escalation notifications delivered within 5 minutes via:

Email to escalated approver
Mobile push notification (if configured)
Executive dashboard alert
System inbox message
Rule 7.4.2: Original Approver Notification
Original approver receives escalation notice:

"Your approval for [request] was escalated to [higher authority] due to [timeout/threshold]"
Includes impact on approver's performance metrics
Repeated escalations trigger supervisor review
Rule 7.4.3: Requestor Notification
Requestor receives status update:

"Your request has been escalated for expedited review"
New expected timeline
No ability to cancel or modify during escalation
7.5 Escalation Analytics
Rule 7.5.1: System tracks escalation rates:

By approver (identifies bottlenecks)
By department (identifies process issues)
By request type (identifies policy problems)
By time of day (identifies capacity issues)
Rule 7.5.2: Escalation reports reviewed monthly:

Approvers with >10% escalation rate flagged for training
Request types with >20% escalation rate reviewed for threshold adjustment
Departments with excessive escalation receive process audit
Rule 7.5.3: Chronic escalation patterns indicate governance configuration problems:

Thresholds may be too restrictive
Approval timelines may be unrealistic
Approver capacity may be insufficient
Policy clarity may be lacking
SECTION 8: OVERRIDE HANDLING IN WORKFLOWS
Overrides are governance exceptions. Workflows must treat overrides as high-risk actions requiring enhanced controls.

8.1 Override as Explicit Workflow Step
Rule 8.1.1: Overrides cannot be hidden workflow branches. Override paths must be explicit, named, and separately audited.

Example:

❌ Invalid: Workflow silently routes $60K PO to Manager when Director unavailable
✅ Valid: Workflow presents "Request Emergency Override" button, requires Director+COO dual approval
Rule 8.1.2: Override workflow steps must be visually distinct in workflow diagrams and labeled "OVERRIDE" or "EXCEPTION PATH."

Rule 8.1.3: Override paths must require higher authority than normal path (Phase 1.1 Section 6.3.2):

Normal: Manager approval → Override: Director approval
Normal: Director approval → Override: CFO/COO approval
Normal: CFO approval → Override: CEO approval
8.2 Override Justification Requirements
Rule 8.2.1: Override requests must capture:

Why normal process cannot be followed (minimum 100 characters)
Business impact if override not granted (quantified if possible)
Alternatives considered and why rejected
Expected duration (for temporary overrides)
Risk assessment and mitigation plan
Rule 8.2.2: Insufficient justification prevents workflow progression. System validates minimum character count, prohibits placeholder text (e.g., "urgent", "needed ASAP").

Rule 8.2.3: Justification is immutable after submission. Cannot edit justification during approval process.

8.3 Override Approval Authority
Rule 8.3.1: Override approval requires authority TWO levels above normal requirement (Phase 1.1 Section 6.3.2).

Rule 8.3.2: Override approval cannot be delegated. If override approver unavailable, request waits or is rejected—no automatic escalation to peer level.

Rule 8.3.3: Self-approval of overrides is absolutely prohibited. Approver cannot approve their own override request under any circumstances.

8.4 Override Audit Severity
Rule 8.4.1: All overrides are logged as "Medium" governance events minimum (Phase 1.1 Section 9.1).

Rule 8.4.2: Overrides exceeding $50K or affecting material financial/operational outcomes are automatically "High" severity.

Rule 8.4.3: Override patterns (same user >3 overrides in 30 days) automatically escalate to "High" severity and trigger investigation.

8.5 Executive Override Visibility
Rule 8.5.1: All overrides appear on executive dashboard within 1 hour:

CFO sees all financial overrides
COO sees all operational overrides
CEO sees all overrides >$100K or strategic impact
Rule 8.5.2: Monthly override report delivered to executives:

Override frequency by department
Override approval velocity by approver
Override success rate (approved vs. rejected)
Override pattern analysis
Rule 8.5.3: Excessive overrides (>5% of transactions in any department) trigger governance review:

Are policies too restrictive?
Are thresholds unrealistic?
Is training inadequate?
Is there circumvention behavior?
8.6 Prohibited Override Patterns
Pattern 8.6.1: Silent Override Paths
Workflow cannot implement conditional logic that bypasses approval requirements without explicit override step.

Example Violation: "If Director unavailable for >4 hours, route to Manager" → This is silent downgrade, not explicit override.

Pattern 8.6.2: Automatic Override
Workflow cannot auto-approve override requests based on historical patterns, trust scores, or AI/ML models.

Pattern 8.6.3: Standing Overrides
Workflow cannot implement "pre-approved override" for specific users or situations. Each override requires case-by-case approval.

Pattern 8.6.4: Override Chaining
Workflow cannot allow multiple sequential overrides (e.g., override threshold, then override approval authority). Single override per request maximum.

SECTION 9: WORKFLOW AUDIT REQUIREMENTS
Every workflow must capture complete audit trail. Workflows without audit compliance are invalid and cannot be deployed.

9.1 Mandatory Audit Data Elements
Every workflow execution must capture:

Element 9.1.1: Requestor Identity

User ID and full name
Department and role
IP address and location
Timestamp of request initiation
Device type (desktop/mobile/API)
Element 9.1.2: Request Details

Request type and unique identifier
Affected entities (customer, order, inventory item, etc.)
Amounts and quantities
Policy threshold applicable
Authority level required
Element 9.1.3: Approval Chain

Each approver in sequence
Timestamp of each approval/rejection
Approval authority level validated
Time elapsed per approval step
Delegation if applicable

9.2 Audit Trail Characteristics
Characteristic 9.2.1: Immutability
Workflow audit data is immutable once written. Cannot be edited or deleted by any user role including system administrators (Phase 1.1 Section 8).

Characteristic 9.2.2: Completeness
Audit trail must capture every step of workflow execution. No gaps. Failed attempts and rejected requests are logged with equal detail to approved requests.

Characteristic 9.2.3: Timeliness
Audit records written in real-time (within 1 second of event). No batch logging or delayed writes.

Characteristic 9.2.4: Integrity
Audit records include hash or checksum to detect tampering. Any modification attempt creates tamper-evidence log entry.

Characteristic 9.2.5: Accessibility
Audit trail accessible to authorized parties:

Internal auditors: Full read access
External auditors: Full read access with export capability
Compliance officers: Full read access
Executives: Dashboard summary with drill-down
Users: Own request history only
Characteristic 9.2.6: Retention
Workflow audit data retained for:

Active workflows: Indefinitely
Completed workflows: 7 years minimum (compliance requirement)
Rejected workflows: 3 years minimum
Override workflows: 10 years minimum (enhanced scrutiny)

SECTION 10: FAILURE & EXCEPTION RULES
Workflow systems must fail safely. When failures occur, system behavior must protect governance integrity.

10.1 Fail Safe, Not Fail Open
Principle: When workflow system encounters error, ambiguity, or missing configuration, system must default to most restrictive behavior (fail safe), not most permissive behavior (fail open).

Fail Safe Behaviors:

If approver authority unclear: Escalate to next level
If threshold ambiguous: Apply most restrictive threshold
If approval timeout unclear: Escalate immediately
If segregation of duties uncertain: Require additional approval
If override path invalid: Reject request with explanation
If audit logging fails: Block workflow execution until logging restored
Fail Open Behaviors (Prohibited):

❌ Auto-approve if approver unavailable
❌ Default to lower threshold if higher threshold unclear
❌ Allow self-approval if normal approver unavailable
❌ Skip audit logging if system busy
❌ Proceed with execution if validation fails
10.2 Workflow Misconfiguration Handling
Rule 10.2.1: Missing Approver
If workflow attempts to route to role that has no assigned users:

Workflow pauses with error status
System admin notified immediately
Request escalates to higher authority automatically after 4 hours
Workflow cannot proceed until approver assigned or escalation completes
Rule 10.2.2: Invalid Threshold Reference
If workflow references threshold that doesn't exist or is ambiguous:

Workflow blocks with configuration error
System admin notified immediately
Request queues for manual routing by system admin
Workflow administrator credential suspended until remediated
Rule 10.2.3: Circular Approval Loop
If workflow creates circular approval path (A approves, routes to B, routes back to A):

Workflow detects loop and terminates with error
Request escalates to executive level (CFO/COO)
Workflow configuration flagged for immediate correction
All workflows using same template suspended until validated
Rule 10.2.4: Authority Conflict
If multiple approval paths yield conflicting authority requirements:

System applies most restrictive (highest authority required)
Workflow logs ambiguity as configuration defect
Request completes under most restrictive path
Configuration reviewed and corrected before next execution
10.3 Approver Unavailability Handling
Rule 10.3.1: Planned Absence (Vacation, Travel)
Approvers must pre-configure delegation before absence:

Delegate must have equal or greater authority
Delegation time-limited (start and end dates)
Delegation logged and auditable
Delegation cannot be retroactive
Rule 10.3.2: Unplanned Absence (Illness, Emergency)
If approver unavailable and no delegation configured:

Workflow waits for normal timeout period (24-72 hours per action type)
After timeout, automatic escalation to next authority level
No automatic approval or peer delegation
Original approver notified upon return of escalation
Rule 10.3.3: Permanent Departure (Termination, Resignation)
When approver leaves organization:

All pending approvals immediately escalate to approver's manager
User's approval authority revoked simultaneously with termination
Workflow system prevents terminated user from approving (even if logged in through active session)
Rule 10.3.4: Executive Unavailability
CFO, COO, CEO unavailability:

Executive approvals cannot be delegated (except CEO to COO+CFO jointly)
Requests wait for executive return or timeout
Material requests (>$500K) escalate to board level if executive unavailable >72 hours
Emergency protocols require board chair approval for critical decisions
10.4 Conflicting Approvals (Parallel Approval Paths)
Rule 10.4.1: Approval Conflict
When workflow requires multiple approvers (e.g., Finance + Operations) and they disagree:

Request is rejected (conservative default)
Both approvers notified of conflict
Requestor notified of rejection with conflict explanation
Escalation to both approvers' common manager for resolution
Cannot proceed without conflict resolution
Rule 10.4.2: Partial Approval
When workflow requires multiple approvals and some approve, some pending:

Request remains in pending state
No partial execution
Pending approvers receive escalation reminder
Timeout applies to slowest approver
Rule 10.4.3: Sequential Approval Reversal
If higher authority rejects after lower authority approved:

Request rejected (higher authority prevails)
Lower authority notified of reversal
Pattern tracked (repeated reversals indicate threshold misalignment)
10.5 System Failure Handling
Rule 10.5.1: Workflow Engine Downtime
During workflow system outage:

No new workflow requests accepted
Pending approvals preserved in queue
Timeout clocks pause during outage
Upon restoration, all pending workflows reviewed for staleness
Material workflows (>$50K) receive executive notification of delay
Rule 10.5.2: Audit System Downtime
During audit logging system outage:

Workflow engine halts immediately
No workflows execute without audit capability
Pending workflows remain in queue
Upon audit restoration, workflows resume with gap analysis
Any missing audit data triggers investigation
Rule 10.5.3: Database Inconsistency
If workflow database state becomes inconsistent (e.g., approved but not executed, executed but not logged):

Inconsistent workflows flagged for manual review
System admin and internal audit notified
Affected workflows suspended until state reconciled
Reconciliation process audited and documented
10.6 Exception Escalation to Humans
Rule 10.6.1: When workflow encounters unresolvable exception:

System does not guess or assume
Request escalates to human decision-maker (typically system admin or compliance officer)
Exception details captured in audit trail
Human resolution becomes precedent for future configuration
Rule 10.6.2: Frequent exceptions (>10 of same type in 30 days) indicate:

Workflow configuration inadequate
Business process changed but workflow not updated
Governance rules unclear or contradictory
Triggers governance review and workflow redesign

CLOSING STATEMENT: WORKFLOW EXISTS TO PROTECT THE BUSINESS, NOT TO ACCELERATE MISTAKES
The fundamental truth about workflow automation:

Workflow engines are extraordinarily powerful. They can route decisions, enforce policies, capture justifications, and scale approval processes across thousands of users and millions of transactions.

But power without boundaries is risk.

The danger of unconstrained workflows:

It is trivially easy to build a workflow that "streamlines" approvals by:

Auto-approving requests from "trusted users"
Bypassing checks when "time is critical"
Reducing approval requirements for "low-risk" transactions
Silently escalating to lower authority when higher authority unavailable
Every one of these patterns is a governance violation waiting to become a regulatory finding, an audit failure, or a fraud scheme.

The discipline of bounded workflows:

This document establishes absolute boundaries:

Workflow enforces governance; it does not define governance
Workflow routes decisions; it does not make decisions
Workflow captures evidence; it does not hide accountability
Workflow fails safely; it does not fail open
These boundaries are non-negotiable because workflow exists to protect the business, not to accelerate mistakes.

Fast is not better if it's wrong:

A workflow that approves a $100,000 purchase order in 30 seconds by bypassing proper authority is not efficient—it's dangerous. A workflow that auto-approves expense claims because the approver is on vacation is not helpful—it's a control failure.

The right speed for a governed action is: As fast as proper authority can be obtained. No faster.

The trust equation:

Enterprise buyers—CFOs, COOs, audit committees—trust systems that slow down bad decisions, not systems that accelerate all decisions.

They trust systems that:

Force justification before approval
Require higher authority for exceptions
Escalate anomalies for human review
Preserve complete audit trails
Surface patterns of concerning behavior
They distrust systems that:

Optimize for speed at the expense of control
Hide overrides in conditional logic
Bypass approvals when convenient
Minimize documentation requirements
Suppress governance warnings
The operational covenant:

By implementing workflow automation within the boundaries defined in this document, we make a covenant with our customers:

"Our workflow engine will never help you violate your own policies. It will never hide accountability. It will never optimize for convenience at the expense of control. It will enforce your governance rules even when enforcement is inconvenient. Because that is what enterprise control systems do."

This is not a limitation of the workflow engine. This is its primary value proposition.

The competitive differentiator:

Competitors will build workflow engines that are "flexible," "user-friendly," and "fast." These are code words for "inadequately governed."

We build workflow engines that are trustworthy. Trustworthiness comes from boundaries, not flexibility. From enforcement, not convenience. From audit trails, not speed.

When we demonstrate our workflow engine to a CFO, we will say:

"Our workflows cannot bypass your authority rules. They cannot hide overrides. They cannot skip audit trails. They cannot auto-approve governed actions. This is intentional. This is how we protect your business."

And the CFO will recognize: This is a vendor who understands governance.

The final principle:

Workflow automation is a tool for scaling governance, not circumventing it. The faster we can execute governed actions safely, the more value we deliver. But "safely" is not optional. "Safely" is the requirement.

Workflow exists to protect the business, not to accelerate mistakes.
