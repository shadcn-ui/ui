## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document defines the **mandatory framework for Board of Directors and investor oversight of autonomous operations**. It specifies what information the Board receives, how often, what decisions the Board must make, and how autonomy affects fiduciary duties.

**This is a governance and accountability framework.**  
**This is not a technical architecture document.**  
**This is not a product roadmap.**  
**This is not a developer guide.**

**Purpose:**  
Enable the Board and investors to fulfill their fiduciary duties while the organization deploys autonomous systems that act without case-by-case human approval.

**Core Question the Board Must Answer:**  
**"Are autonomous operations creating value or creating risk?"**

If the Board cannot answer this question with confidence, the oversight framework has failed.

**Board's Fiduciary Obligations:**
1. **Duty of Care:** Understand what autonomy is, how it works, what risks it creates
2. **Duty of Loyalty:** Ensure autonomy serves organizational interests, not management convenience
3. **Duty of Oversight:** Monitor autonomy performance, detect problems early, intervene when necessary
4. **Duty of Disclosure:** Ensure investors receive accurate information about autonomy risks and performance

**Key Governance Principles:**
- **Transparency:** Board receives complete, accurate information (no surprises)
- **Simplicity:** Reports use plain language (no technical jargon)
- **Actionability:** Reports enable decisions (not just information dumps)
- **Accountability:** Clear ownership for autonomy outcomes (executives cannot hide behind "the AI did it")
- **Escalation:** Board knows when to intervene (automatic escalation triggers defined)

**What the Board Will Receive:**
1. **Quarterly Autonomy Oversight Report** (15-20 pages, plain language)
2. **Annual Autonomy Risk Assessment** (comprehensive risk review)
3. **Incident Briefings** (within 24 hours for CLASS 3-4 incidents)
4. **Major Decision Approvals** (Tier 3â†’4 promotions, global kill-switch activations, external Tier 1 integrations)

**What the Board Will NOT Receive:**
- Daily operational metrics (that's management's job)
- Technical implementation details (Board is not an engineering review committee)
- Individual transaction logs (Board oversees governance, not operations)

**Red Flags Requiring Immediate Board Attention:**
1. Global autonomy kill-switch activated
2. CLASS 4 incident (systemic autonomy failure)
3. External system security breach affecting autonomy
4. Regulatory inquiry related to autonomous operations
5. Autonomy-related lawsuit or customer harm claim
6. Executive conflict over autonomy governance

## PURPOSE & PHILOSOPHY

### 1.1 Why Board Oversight of Autonomy Is Non-Negotiable

**Traditional business operations:**
- Humans make decisions
- Humans can be questioned, corrected, held accountable
- Board oversees humans (CEO â†’ executives â†’ managers â†’ employees)

**Autonomous operations:**
- Systems make decisions without case-by-case human approval
- Systems cannot be questioned in traditional sense
- Board must oversee systems + humans who deploy systems

**Risk:** If Board does not understand autonomy, Board cannot fulfill fiduciary duty.

**Therefore:** Autonomy oversight is not optional. It is a fiduciary requirement.

---

### 1.2 The Board's Role Is NOT Technical Review

**Board members are not:**
- Software engineers
- AI experts
- Technical architects

**Board members ARE:**
- Fiduciaries responsible for organizational success
- Risk overseers
- Strategic advisors
- Investor representatives

**Therefore:** Board oversight focuses on **risk, strategy, and accountability**, not technical implementation.

**Board asks:**
- "Is autonomy creating value?" (Strategic question)
- "Is autonomy creating risk?" (Risk question)
- "Who is accountable if autonomy fails?" (Accountability question)

**Board does NOT ask:**
- "What ML algorithm powers the recommendation engine?" (Technical detail)
- "How many API calls per second?" (Operational metric)

---

### 1.3 Autonomy as Enterprise Risk

**Autonomy creates new risk categories:**

**Operational Risk:**
- Autonomous systems make wrong decisions at scale
- Errors compound faster than humans can detect/correct
- Loss of human judgment in critical processes

**Financial Risk:**
- Autonomous systems execute unauthorized transactions
- Financial exposure grows silently (e.g., inventory over-ordering)
- Revenue/margin impact from autonomous pricing errors

**Compliance Risk:**
- Autonomous systems violate regulations without human awareness
- Audit trails incomplete or non-existent
- Liability for "black box" decisions

**Reputational Risk:**
- Customer harm from autonomous errors (e.g., incorrect invoicing, delivery failures)
- Public perception: "Company let robots make bad decisions"
- Loss of customer trust

**Strategic Risk:**
- Over-automation creates organizational brittleness (no humans who understand processes)
- Competitive disadvantage if autonomy fails (manual operations cannot scale)
- Vendor lock-in (dependence on external AI systems)

**Board's oversight responsibility:** Ensure management is managing these risks, not creating them.

---

### 1.4 What This Document Governs

**In Scope:**
- Board reporting framework (what reports, how often, what content)
- Board decision authority (what decisions require Board approval)
- Escalation triggers (when does management notify Board immediately)
- Investor communication (what investors are told about autonomy)
- Fiduciary duty considerations (how Board demonstrates duty of care/oversight)

**Out of Scope:**
- Technical implementation (covered by Phases 1-4)
- Daily operations (management's responsibility)
- Individual employee performance (HR/management responsibility)

## BOARD RESPONSIBILITIES & AUTHORITY

### 2.1 Board's Three-Tiered Oversight Model

**Tier 1: Approval Authority (Board Decides)**

Board must approve:
1. **Autonomy Strategy:** Overall approach to autonomous operations (aggressive vs conservative)
2. **Forbidden Autonomy List:** Which actions are never automated (Phase 3.1 list)
3. **External Tier 1 Integrations:** Critical external systems with financial/compliance access
4. **Tier 3â†’4 Promotions:** Any capability graduating to self-tuning autonomy
5. **Autonomy Budget:** Annual spending on autonomous systems (development, vendors, monitoring)

**Board vote required.** Cannot be delegated to management.

---

**Tier 2: Oversight & Challenge (Board Questions, Management Executes)**

Board oversees and may challenge:
1. **Quarterly Autonomy Performance:** Is autonomy delivering expected value?
2. **Incident Response:** How did management handle autonomy failures?
3. **Risk Management:** Are risk controls adequate?
4. **External Integrations (Tier 2-3):** Are vendors trustworthy?
5. **Promotion/Downgrade Trends:** Is autonomy expanding too fast or too slow?

**Board can request changes, additional safeguards, or pause expansion.** Final execution by management.

---

**Tier 3: Information Only (Board Informed, Management Executes)**

Board is informed but does not intervene:
1. **Operational Metrics:** Daily/weekly operational performance
2. **Minor Incidents (CLASS 1):** Routine operational issues
3. **Tier 1â†’2 Promotions:** Low-risk autonomy graduation
4. **Vendor Performance:** SLA compliance, operational metrics

**Board trusts management to execute.** If patterns emerge (e.g., persistent incidents), Board escalates to Tier 2 oversight.

---

### 2.2 Audit Committee's Enhanced Role

**The Audit Committee has special autonomy oversight responsibilities:**

**Quarterly Audit Committee Review:**
1. **Audit Trail Integrity:** Is Phase 1.3 audit framework working? (Are autonomous actions auditable?)
2. **Compliance Risk:** Are autonomous systems creating regulatory exposure?
3. **External Auditor Perspective:** What do external auditors think of autonomy controls?
4. **Incident Review:** CLASS 2+ incidents (detailed review)
5. **Internal Audit Findings:** Internal Audit's assessment of autonomy governance compliance

**Why Audit Committee?**
- Autonomy creates audit/compliance risks
- Audit Committee has expertise in internal controls
- Audit Committee works closely with external auditors (who must audit autonomous transactions)

**Audit Committee can:**
- Request external audit of autonomy controls
- Require management to strengthen controls
- Escalate concerns to full Board
- Recommend suspension of autonomy if controls inadequate

---

### 2.3 Board's Right to Suspend Autonomy

**Board has ultimate authority to suspend autonomy.**

**Board can vote to:**
- Suspend specific domain autonomy (e.g., "No Tier 3+ in Finance domain until controls strengthened")
- Suspend external integrations (e.g., "No new Tier 1 external integrations for 6 months")
- Suspend all Tier 3+ autonomy (e.g., "Downgrade everything to Tier 2 pending risk review")

**When would Board exercise this authority?**
- Management fails to address persistent incidents
- Audit Committee identifies serious control weaknesses
- Regulatory inquiry raises compliance concerns
- Major autonomy-related customer harm or lawsuit

**Board suspension = nuclear option.** Rarely used, but authority must exist.

---

### 2.4 Board Decision Log

**All Board autonomy decisions logged:**
- Date of Board meeting
- Decision made (approve/reject/defer)
- Vote result (unanimous, majority, dissent)
- Rationale (why did Board decide this way?)
- Expected outcome (what does Board expect from this decision?)

**Purpose:**
- Demonstrate Board fulfilled fiduciary duty
- Enable Board to learn from past decisions
- Provide audit trail for investors/regulators

**Storage:** Board Secretary maintains decision log (7+ year retention).

## ANNUAL AUTONOMY RISK ASSESSMENT (DEEP DIVE)

### 4.1 Purpose

**Quarterly reports track operational health. Annual assessment evaluates strategic risk.**

**Annual assessment asks:**
- Is autonomy aligned with organizational strategy?
- Are we automating the right things?
- Are risk controls adequate for future growth?
- What emerging risks need Board attention?

---

### 4.2 Annual Assessment Content

**Section 1: Strategic Alignment Review**

**Question:** Is autonomy supporting strategic goals or distracting from them?

**Content:**
- What strategic goals did organization set 12 months ago?
- How did autonomy contribute to (or hinder) those goals?
- What strategic goals exist for next 12 months?
- How should autonomy strategy adjust?

**Board outcome:** Confirm autonomy strategy still aligned with business strategy.

---

**Section 2: Autonomy Maturity Assessment**

**Question:** How mature is organization's autonomy capability?

**Maturity levels:**
- **Level 1 (Initial):** Ad-hoc autonomy, no governance framework
- **Level 2 (Developing):** Governance framework exists, inconsistently applied
- **Level 3 (Defined):** Governance framework consistently applied, metrics tracked
- **Level 4 (Managed):** Data-driven optimization, predictive risk management
- **Level 5 (Optimizing):** Continuous improvement, industry-leading practices

**Assessment:** Where is organization today? Where should it be in 12 months?

**Board outcome:** Understand organizational readiness for autonomy expansion.

---

**Section 3: Risk Deep Dive**

**Question:** What are the top 5 autonomy-related risks?

**Content:**
- Risk description (what could go wrong?)
- Risk likelihood (how likely is this to occur?)
- Risk impact (what would be the harm?)
- Current mitigations (what controls exist?)
- Residual risk (what risk remains after mitigations?)
- Board decision needed (should Board require additional controls?)

**Example:**
```
RISK: Over-reliance on single external AI vendor

Description: Organization uses External Vendor X for 80% of autonomous 
recommendations. If vendor fails, experiences outage, or terminates contract, 
organization loses critical autonomy capability.

Likelihood: Moderate (vendors can fail, contracts can end)
Impact: High (manual operations cannot scale to handle workload)

Current Mitigations:
â€¢ Multi-year contract with vendor (expires Dec 2027)
â€¢ SLA requires 99.9% uptime
â€¢ Contractual penalty for SLA breaches

Residual Risk: Moderate-High

Recommendation: Develop internal capability or identify backup vendor to reduce 
single-vendor dependency.

Board Decision Needed: Approve $500K investment in internal AI capability development?
```

**Board outcome:** Board understands top risks and approves/rejects risk mitigation investments.

---

**Section 4: External Autonomy Review**

**Question:** Are external systems creating unacceptable risk?

**Content:**
- How many external integrations? (Tier 1, 2, 3, 4)
- Any vendor security breaches this year?
- Any contract disputes or SLA failures?
- Vendor concentration risk (over-reliance on specific vendors?)
- Recommendation: Continue, reduce, or expand external integrations?

**Board outcome:** Board approves external integration strategy for next 12 months.

---

**Section 5: Regulatory & Compliance Outlook**

**Question:** What regulatory changes could affect autonomy?

**Content:**
- New regulations proposed or enacted (e.g., AI regulation, algorithmic transparency laws)
- How would new regulations affect organization's autonomy?
- What changes needed to maintain compliance?
- Financial impact of compliance (investment required?)

**Board outcome:** Board understands regulatory risk and approves compliance investments.

---

**Section 6: Peer Benchmarking**

**Question:** How does organization's autonomy compare to peers?

**Content:**
- Industry average: % of operations automated
- Organization's autonomy penetration (higher or lower than peers?)
- Industry best practices (what are leading companies doing?)
- Competitive implications (does autonomy create advantage or disadvantage?)

**Board outcome:** Board assesses whether organization is leading, lagging, or appropriately positioned.

---

**Section 7: Three-Year Autonomy Roadmap**

**Question:** Where is autonomy heading in next 3 years?

**Content:**
- Current state (today)
- Year 1 goals (next 12 months)
- Year 2-3 vision (longer-term direction)
- Investment required (annual spending forecast)
- Expected ROI (value delivery projections)

**Board outcome:** Board approves (or adjusts) multi-year autonomy roadmap.

## INCIDENT ESCALATION TO BOARD (WHEN THINGS GO WRONG)

### 5.1 Purpose

**Board cannot wait for quarterly report if critical incident occurs.**

**Immediate Board notification required for:**
1. CLASS 3-4 autonomy incidents
2. Global autonomy kill-switch activation
3. External system security breach (affecting autonomy)
4. Regulatory inquiry related to autonomy
5. Autonomy-related lawsuit or customer harm claim
6. Executive conflict over autonomy governance (e.g., CTO vs CFO disagreement on risk acceptance)

---

### 5.2 Incident Notification Process

**For CLASS 3 incidents:**
- **Timing:** Board Chair + Audit Committee Chair notified within 24 hours
- **Method:** Email or phone call from CEO
- **Content:** Brief summary (2 paragraphs: what happened, what we're doing)

**For CLASS 4 incidents:**
- **Timing:** Board Chair + Audit Committee Chair notified within 2 hours
- **Method:** Phone call from CEO (no email-only notification)
- **Content:** Brief verbal summary, full written briefing within 24 hours

**For global kill-switch activation:**
- **Timing:** Board Chair notified immediately (<1 hour)
- **Method:** Phone call from CEO or CTO
- **Content:** Why was kill-switch activated? When will autonomy resume?

---

### 5.3 Incident Briefing Content

**Initial notification (within 24 hours):**
- **What happened:** Brief incident description (plain language)
- **Impact:** Financial, operational, compliance, customer impact
- **Response:** What did management do? (kill-switch, downgrade, investigation)
- **Status:** Is incident contained? Resolved? Ongoing?
- **Next steps:** What will management do next? When will Board receive full report?

**Follow-up report (within 7 days):**
- **Detailed timeline:** Chronological incident sequence
- **Root cause analysis:** Why did incident occur?
- **Remediation:** What changed to prevent recurrence?
- **Lessons learned:** What did organization learn?
- **Board decision needed:** Does Board need to take action? (e.g., suspend autonomy expansion)

---

### 5.4 Emergency Board Meeting

**For CLASS 4 incidents or major crises, Board Chair may convene emergency Board meeting.**

**Emergency meeting process:**
- **Convening:** Board Chair (or CEO) requests emergency meeting
- **Notice:** 48 hours notice (or less if truly urgent)
- **Agenda:** Incident briefing + Board decision (continue/suspend autonomy)
- **Quorum:** Majority of Board members required

**Emergency meeting outcomes:**
- Board affirms management response (continue as planned)
- Board requires additional safeguards (before resuming autonomy)
- Board suspends autonomy (temporarily or indefinitely)

## BOARD DECISION AUTHORITIES (WHAT BOARD MUST APPROVE)

### 6.1 Tier 3â†’4 Promotion Approval

**Any capability graduating to Tier 4 (self-tuning autonomy) requires Board vote.**

**Why Board approval?**
- Tier 4 = system adjusts its own parameters
- Highest autonomy level, highest risk
- Organizational experience with Tier 4 limited

**Management provides Board with:**
- Capability description (what does it do?)
- Business justification (why self-tuning needed?)
- Risk assessment (what could go wrong?)
- Safeguards (what controls exist?)
- Performance track record (12+ months at Tier 3, zero incidents)

**Board votes:** Approve / Approve with conditions / Defer / Reject

**If Board rejects:** Capability remains at Tier 3, management can resubmit after addressing Board concerns.

---

### 6.2 External Tier 1 Integration Approval

**Any new Tier 1 external integration (critical external systems) requires Board vote.**

**Why Board approval?**
- Tier 1 = external system has write access to financial/compliance data
- High risk of vendor breach or violation
- Potential regulatory exposure

**Management provides Board with:**
- Vendor description (who are they? reputation?)
- Integration purpose (why is this vendor necessary?)
- Risk assessment (what could go wrong?)
- Contract terms (liability, termination rights, SLAs)
- Security review (CISO assessment of vendor security)

**Board votes:** Approve / Approve with conditions / Defer / Reject

**If Board rejects:** Integration does not proceed, management seeks alternative vendor or internal solution.

---

### 6.3 Annual Autonomy Budget Approval

**Board approves annual budget for autonomy investments.**

**Budget categories:**
- **Development:** Engineering time, internal development
- **Vendors:** SaaS platforms, AI APIs, external integrations
- **Monitoring & Tools:** Observability, logging, dashboards
- **Governance:** Audit, compliance, training

**Management proposes budget (as part of annual budget cycle).**

**Board approves budget (may adjust up or down).**

**Mid-year adjustments:** If management needs >20% budget increase, Board approval required.

---

### 6.4 Autonomy Strategy Changes

**Major changes to autonomy strategy require Board approval.**

**What constitutes "major change"?**
- Shifting from conservative to aggressive autonomy posture (or vice versa)
- Expanding Forbidden Autonomy List (removing items from forbidden list)
- Changing risk tolerance (e.g., accepting higher confidence score thresholds)
- Outsourcing critical autonomy to external vendors

**Management proposes strategy change.**

**Board evaluates:**
- Why is change needed?
- What are the risks?
- What are the expected benefits?
- How will change be implemented?

**Board votes:** Approve / Approve with modifications / Defer / Reject

## INVESTOR COMMUNICATION (WHAT EXTERNAL STAKEHOLDERS HEAR)

### 7.1 Purpose

**Investors (equity holders, debt holders, prospective investors) need transparency about autonomy.**

**Investors care about:**
- Is autonomy creating value? (ROI, cost savings, revenue growth)
- Is autonomy creating risk? (operational failures, compliance exposure)
- Is autonomy governance adequate? (Board oversight, management accountability)

**Investors do NOT need:**
- Technical implementation details
- Daily operational metrics
- Proprietary algorithmic information

---

### 7.2 Quarterly Investor Updates (Public Companies)

**For public companies, autonomy mentioned in quarterly earnings releases and calls:**

**Earnings release (written):**
- Brief mention in management discussion (1-2 paragraphs)
- Focus on value delivery (cost savings, efficiency gains)
- Mention any material incidents (if financially significant)

**Example:**
```
"During Q1 2026, the Company continued expanding autonomous operations in inventory 
management and order fulfillment. Autonomous inventory reordering delivered $125K in 
cost savings through reduced manual processing and improved inventory efficiency. 
The Company maintains robust governance controls and experienced no material autonomy-
related incidents during the quarter."
```

**Earnings call (verbal):**
- CFO mentions autonomy in prepared remarks (if material)
- Q&A: CFO + CEO answer investor questions about autonomy (if asked)

**Guidance:**
- If autonomy is significant value driver, highlight it
- If autonomy is not yet material, mention briefly (demonstrate transparency)
- If autonomy incident occurred, address proactively (don't hide)

- Autonomy strategy overview (high-level)

### 7.4 Material Incident Disclosure

**If autonomy incident is financially material, disclosure required:**

**For public companies:**
- 8-K filing (if incident affects financial results or creates legal liability)
- Earnings release/call (mention incident and impact)

**For private companies:**
- Immediate notification to investors (per shareholder agreement)
- Detailed briefing at next Board meeting

**What is "material"?**
- Financial impact >$1M (or 1% of revenue, whichever lower)
- Regulatory inquiry or enforcement action
- Lawsuit filed by customer or third party
- Reputational harm (negative press coverage)

**Disclosure principles:**
- Be transparent (don't hide bad news)
- Be factual (state what happened, not speculation)
- Explain response (what did management do?)
- Provide context (isolated incident vs systemic issue?)

## FIDUCIARY DUTY CONSIDERATIONS (BOARD LIABILITY PROTECTION)

### 8.1 Board's Fiduciary Duties Recap

**Board of Directors owes fiduciary duties to shareholders:**

**Duty of Care:**
- Act with care a reasonably prudent person would exercise
- Stay informed about organizational operations (including autonomy)
- Make decisions based on adequate information

**Duty of Loyalty:**
- Act in shareholders' best interests, not personal interests
- Ensure autonomy serves organizational goals, not management convenience

**Duty of Oversight:**
- Monitor organizational operations
- Detect problems early (red flags)
- Intervene when necessary

**Duty of Good Faith:**
- Act honestly and with integrity
- Not intentionally disregard known risks

---

### 8.2 How Board Demonstrates Duty of Care (Autonomy Context)

**Board fulfills duty of care by:**

1. **Receiving regular reports:** Quarterly Autonomy Oversight Report demonstrates Board is informed
2. **Asking questions:** Board minutes show Board actively questioned management about autonomy
3. **Requesting expert advice:** Board may retain consultants for autonomy risk assessment (demonstrates diligence)
4. **Approving major decisions:** Board vote on Tier 3â†’4 promotions, external Tier 1 integrations demonstrates Board oversight
5. **Documenting decisions:** Board decision log shows rationale for decisions (not arbitrary)

**If Board is sued (derivative action or shareholder lawsuit):**
- Board can point to quarterly reports, annual assessments, meeting minutes as evidence of care
- Business judgment rule protects Board if Board was informed and acted reasonably

---

### 8.3 Red Flags That Board Must Not Ignore

**If Board becomes aware of these red flags and does nothing, Board may face liability:**

**Red Flag 1: Persistent Incidents Without Remediation**
- If multiple CLASS 2+ incidents occur and management does not downgrade autonomy or strengthen controls
- Board must challenge management: "Why are incidents recurring?"

**Red Flag 2: Management Overconfidence**
- If management dismisses autonomy risks or resists governance controls
- Board must insist on adequate safeguards

**Red Flag 3: Audit Findings Ignored**
- If Internal Audit or external auditors raise autonomy concerns and management does not address
- Board must require remediation

**Red Flag 4: Regulatory Inquiry**
- If regulator asks questions about autonomy and organization does not respond adequately
- Board must ensure compliance

**Red Flag 5: Executive Conflict**
- If CTO and CFO disagree about autonomy risk and conflict is not resolved
- Board must mediate and make decision

**If Board ignores red flags:**
- Shareholders may sue Board for breach of fiduciary duty
- Board may be personally liable for damages (D&O insurance may not cover)

### 8.4 Board Minutes & Documentation

**Board Secretary must document autonomy discussions in Board minutes:**

**Minutes should show:**
- Management presented Quarterly Autonomy Oversight Report

**Why documentation matters:**
- In litigation, Board minutes are primary evidence of Board diligence

- Poor documentation suggests Board was asleep at the wheel

**Best practice:** Have legal counsel review Board minutes before finalization (especially for autonomy discussions).

## CLOSING STATEMENT

Autonomy is powerful. Autonomy is risky. The Board's job is to ensure autonomy is governed.

This oversight framework exists to enable the Board to fulfill its fiduciary duties while the organization deploys systems that act autonomously.

**The Board's fundamental question:**  
**"Are autonomous operations creating value or creating risk?"**

If the Board can confidently answer "Creating value with acceptable risk," the oversight framework is working.

If the Board cannot answer this question, the oversight framework has failed.

**Key principles for Board oversight:**

1. **Transparency:** Board receives complete, accurate information (management does not hide problems)
2. **Simplicity:** Reports use plain language (Board is not a technical review committee)
3. **Actionability:** Reports enable decisions (not just information dumps)
4. **Accountability:** Clear ownership for autonomy outcomes (executives cannot blame "the AI")
5. **Escalation:** Board knows when to intervene (automatic triggers for Board notification)

**Board's oversight tools:**
- **Quarterly Autonomy Oversight Report** (operational health tracking)
- **Annual Autonomy Risk Assessment** (strategic risk evaluation)
- **Incident escalation** (immediate notification of critical issues)
- **Decision authority** (Board approves high-risk autonomy changes)
- **Investor communication** (transparency to external stakeholders)

**Board's ultimate authority:**
- Board can suspend autonomy at any time
- Board can require additional safeguards
- Board can reject management autonomy proposals
- Board can remove executives who create ungoverned autonomy risk

**Fiduciary duty fulfillment:**
- Board demonstrates duty of care by staying informed (quarterly reports, questions, documentation)
- Board demonstrates duty of oversight by monitoring performance and intervening when necessary
- Board demonstrates duty of loyalty by ensuring autonomy serves shareholder interests

**The goal is not zero autonomy. The goal is governed autonomy.**

Autonomy that creates value, with risks that are understood, monitored, and contained.

Autonomy where humans remain accountable, even when systems act autonomously.

Autonomy where the Board can confidently tell shareholders: "We are overseeing this. We understand the risks. We believe the benefits justify the risks."

**That is Board oversight. That is fiduciary duty. That is governance.**

When autonomous systems act, the Board remains accountable. This oversight framework ensures the Board has the visibility and control to fulfill that accountability.
