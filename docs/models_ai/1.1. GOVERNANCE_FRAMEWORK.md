Ocean ERP Enterprise Operating Platform

Document Classification: FOUNDATIONAL - NON-NEGOTIABLE
Version: 1.0
Effective Date: January 5, 2026
Review Cycle: Annual (or upon material breach)
Authority: Chief Technology Officer + Chief Financial Officer
Distribution: Executive Leadership, Product Council, Engineering Leadership, Solutions Architecture

DOCUMENT PURPOSE & BINDING AUTHORITY
This document establishes the immutable governance framework for the Ocean ERP platform. All system behavior, workflow automation, user interfaces, integrations, and data operations MUST comply with these rules. No product feature, customer customization, or operational convenience may circumvent these principles.

Deviation from this framework requires written approval from CTO and CFO with documented risk assessment and remediation plan.

This is not a feature specification. This is the operational constitution of the platform.

SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE INTENT
1.1 Why Governance Before Features
Enterprise Resource Planning is fundamentally about control, not convenience. A system that cannot enforce authority, preserve audit integrity, and provide executive visibility is operationally dangerous regardless of its feature richness.

The core truth: An ERP without governance is an spreadsheet with a prettier interface—and equally unsuitable for enterprise operations.

The business reality: CFOs do not buy ERPs for dashboards. They buy ERPs because they cannot trust manual processes, informal approvals, and unauditable actions. If this platform cannot guarantee control, it has no enterprise value.

The liability principle: Every uncontrolled action is a liability exposure. Every informal approval is an audit failure. Every silent override is a compliance breach waiting to surface.

1.2 Trust Through Control
Enterprise buyers—CFOs, COOs, audit committees—evaluate ERP systems on a single criterion: Can I trust it?

Trust is not built through features. Trust is built through:

Explicit authority: Every action has a required approval level
Immutable audit: No action can be concealed or retroactively altered
Automatic enforcement: Human discretion is eliminated where policy is clear
Executive visibility: Leadership can answer "what happened, who approved it, why" for any action
Violation transparency: Breaches are immediately visible and escalated
If governance fails, trust fails. If trust fails, the platform fails.

1.3 Phase 1.1 Mission Statement
Mission: Establish the governance layer as the immovable foundation upon which all operational workflows, automation, and business intelligence will be built.

Success Criteria:

Every governed action requires explicit authority
Every override is auditable and visible
Every threshold is enforced without human intervention
Every violation triggers automatic escalation
Executive leadership can demonstrate audit compliance to external auditors without system customization
Failure Mode: If any governed action can occur without proper authority, without audit trail, or without executive visibility, Phase 1.1 has failed and subsequent phases are suspended.

SECTION 2: CORE GOVERNANCE PRINCIPLES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
These principles are absolute and admit no exceptions.

PRINCIPLE 1: Authority Before Action
Rule: No governed action may be executed without explicit authority verification.

Corollary: "I didn't know I needed approval" is not a valid system state. The system must enforce authority requirements before presenting action options.

Application: If a user lacks authority for an action, the system must prevent action initiation, not merely flag it after execution.

PRINCIPLE 2: Explicit Limits
Rule: Every authority level has explicit quantitative or qualitative limits. Authority without limits is operational chaos.

Corollary: "Use your judgment" is not an authority specification. All limits must be numeric, enumerated, or rule-based.

Application: Sales managers may approve discounts up to 15% OR $5,000, whichever is lower. Not "reasonable discounts."

PRINCIPLE 3: Override Is Liability, Not Convenience
Rule: Every override of an automated control is a liability event requiring justification, approval, and permanent audit record.

Corollary: Frequent overrides indicate incorrect control configuration, not user expertise. Patterns of overrides trigger governance review.

Application: If inventory adjustments are routinely overriding system controls, the reorder points are misconfigured—not the users are appropriately exercising judgment.

PRINCIPLE 4: Automatic Escalation
Rule: When authority requirements are not met within defined timeframes, escalation is automatic and non-discretionary.

Corollary: No action may remain in "pending approval" state indefinitely. Timeout equals escalation, not implied approval.

Application: Purchase order approval not completed in 48 hours escalates to next authority level. No manual escalation request required.

PRINCIPLE 5: Silence Equals Decision
Rule: For time-sensitive decisions, explicit response timelines are defined. Upon timeout, a default decision (approve or reject) is executed with escalation notification.

Corollary: "I didn't see the request" is not a valid excuse for decision delay. The system enforces decision velocity.

Application: Expense claim not reviewed in 72 hours: auto-approved if under $500, auto-rejected if over $500 with CFO notification.

PRINCIPLE 6: Segregation of Duties
Rule: Initiation, approval, and execution of high-risk actions must be performed by different individuals.

Corollary: Self-approval is prohibited for all governed actions above operator threshold.

Application: User who creates purchase order cannot approve it. User who processes payment cannot post the GL entry.

PRINCIPLE 7: Immutability After Posting
Rule: Once an action reaches "posted" or "completed" state, it becomes immutable. Corrections are made via reversal and new entry, never via edit.

Corollary: "I made a typo" does not justify editing a posted transaction. Reversals preserve audit integrity.

Application: Posted journal entries, completed payroll runs, closed inventory periods cannot be edited. Ever.

PRINCIPLE 8: Executive Visibility Is Non-Negotiable
Rule: Executive leadership must have real-time visibility into governance violations, pending high-value decisions, and override patterns.

Corollary: "The data is in the system, you just need to export it" is unacceptable. Executive dashboards are first-class system components.

Application: CFO dashboard shows all financial overrides, pending approvals over thresholds, and governance violations—updated in real-time, no manual refresh.

PRINCIPLE 9: Governance Violations Are Logged, Not Hidden
Rule: All governance violations—attempted or successful—are permanently logged and cannot be deleted by any user role including system administrators.

Corollary: Attempted violations (blocked actions) are as important as successful violations (approved overrides) for pattern detection.

Application: User attempts to post journal entry without proper authority: attempt is blocked AND logged with user ID, timestamp, and reason for rejection.

PRINCIPLE 10: No Privilege Escalation Without Audit
Rule: Temporary elevation of user authority (emergency access) requires executive approval, time limitation, justification, and automatic de-escalation.

Corollary: "I need admin access to fix this quickly" is not sufficient justification. Emergency access protocols are pre-defined and audited.

Application: Weekend emergency requiring GL posting authority: System admin can request temporary CFO role for 24 hours maximum with CFO email approval and automatic revocation.

SECTION 3: GOVERNANCE-CONTROLLED OBJECTS
The following business objects are subject to mandatory governance controls. Any action affecting these objects must comply with authority and audit requirements defined in this document.

3.1 Financial Objects (Highest Control Level)
Governed Objects:

Journal entries (creation, posting, reversal)
Cash disbursements (payments, wire transfers)
Cash receipts (payment application, deposits)
Account reconciliations (completion, variance approval)
Period close operations (fiscal period lock/unlock)
Chart of accounts modifications (account creation, deactivation)
Budget allocations (creation, modification, reallocation)
Financial statement adjustments (restatements, reclassifications)
Tax filing data (submission, amendments)
Control Requirement: All financial objects require dual authority (initiator + approver) for amounts exceeding defined thresholds. CFO approval required for material amounts or non-standard transactions.

Immutability Trigger: Financial objects become immutable upon posting or fiscal period close, whichever occurs first.

3.2 Revenue & Pricing Objects (Second-Tier Control)
Governed Objects:

Price overrides (below list price)
Discount authorizations (percentage or absolute)
Payment term exceptions (extended credit)
Credit limit increases (customer credit policy)
Order cancellations (after fulfillment initiation)
Revenue recognition timing (deferred revenue adjustments)
Sales returns outside standard policy (restocking fees, time limits)
Invoice adjustments (credit memos, rebilling)
Control Requirement: Sales operations may approve within defined limits. Material exceptions require sales management or finance approval.

Immutability Trigger: Orders become immutable 7 days after creation or upon shipment. Invoices become immutable upon posting to GL.

3.3 Inventory & Operations Objects (Operational Control)
Governed Objects:

Physical inventory adjustments (cycle counts, shrinkage)
Stock transfers between locations (variance reconciliation)
Inventory value adjustments (cost changes, obsolescence write-offs)
Backdated transactions (any inventory movement with past-dated timestamp)
Work order material consumption variances (scrap, rework)
Purchase order receipt variances (quantity or quality discrepancies)
Production yield variances (expected vs. actual output)
Control Requirement: Operations staff may execute within normal variance thresholds. Material variances require operations management approval. Value adjustments require finance review.

Immutability Trigger: Stock movements become immutable upon posting. Closed production periods cannot be reopened.

3.4 Procurement Objects (Fraud Risk Control)
Governed Objects:

Supplier master data creation (new vendor setup)
Supplier bank account changes (payment routing modification)
Purchase order issuance (commitment of funds)
Emergency purchase orders (bypass normal approval chain)
Supplier payment execution (disbursement finalization)
Purchase price variances (received cost vs. PO cost)
Supplier contract modifications (terms, pricing, volume commitments)
Control Requirement: Procurement staff may create POs within authorization limits. New supplier setup and bank changes require multi-level approval with fraud controls. Payments require segregation from PO creation.

Immutability Trigger: Supplier bank account changes effective immediately and irreversible (require new vendor setup for corrections). Purchase orders become immutable upon receipt or cancellation.

3.5 Human Capital Objects (Privacy & Compliance Control)
Governed Objects:

Employee salary changes (base pay, allowances, deductions)
Payroll run execution (calculation and disbursement)
Retroactive payroll adjustments (backdated pay changes)
Employment status changes (termination, leave of absence)
Personnel file access (viewing or modifying PII)
Performance review overrides (rating changes after finalization)
Leave balance adjustments (accrual corrections)
Control Requirement: HR staff may process within policy. Salary changes require manager and HR director approval. Retroactive adjustments require CFO approval for financial impact. Terminations require multi-level approval and legal review.

Immutability Trigger: Posted payroll runs are immutable. Corrections via adjustment runs only. Employment history changes logged permanently.

3.6 System Administration Objects (Security Control)
Governed Objects:

User role assignments (permission elevation)
Data export operations (bulk data extraction)
System configuration changes (business rules, approval workflows)
Integration credentials (API keys, webhook secrets)
Audit log access (viewing or archiving logs)
Database direct access (bypassing application layer)
Security policy modifications (password rules, MFA requirements)
Control Requirement: System administrators may perform routine maintenance. Security-impacting changes require CTO approval. Audit log access requires compliance officer authorization.

Immutability Trigger: Audit logs are immutable. Security policy changes effective immediately with executive notification.

SECTION 4: GOVERNANCE ROLES
Governance roles define authority levels for decision-making. Roles are orthogonal to job titles—a single individual may hold multiple roles, and authority is evaluated per action based on role assignment.

4.1 Role Hierarchy
OPERATOR (Level 1)

Authority Scope: Execute standard transactions within normal parameters
Cannot: Approve, override controls, or execute non-standard transactions
Examples: Warehouse staff recording stock movements, cashiers processing sales, data entry clerks
SUPERVISOR (Level 2)

Authority Scope: Approve routine transactions up to defined thresholds, resolve minor variances
Cannot: Override material controls, approve own transactions, modify master data
Examples: Warehouse supervisor approving cycle count adjustments, shift lead approving employee time corrections
MANAGER (Level 3)

Authority Scope: Approve material transactions up to department limits, authorize exceptions within policy, modify operational configurations
Cannot: Approve cross-departmental impacts, override financial controls, modify security settings
Examples: Sales manager approving 15% discounts, operations manager approving inventory write-offs up to $10K
DIRECTOR (Level 4)

Authority Scope: Approve transactions with enterprise impact, authorize policy exceptions, modify workflows within domain
Cannot: Override financial immutability, approve multi-department strategic decisions
Examples: Finance director approving GL reclassifications, operations director approving production schedule overhauls
CFO (Level 5 - Financial Authority)

Authority Scope: Final financial authority, approve all material financial transactions and exceptions, unlock closed periods (with audit justification)
Cannot: Override system security controls, execute actions without audit trail
Examples: Approve material journal entries, authorize financial statement adjustments, override credit limits for strategic customers
COO (Level 5 - Operational Authority)

Authority Scope: Final operational authority, approve strategic operational changes, authorize emergency operational overrides
Cannot: Override financial controls without CFO co-approval, modify audit logs
Examples: Approve major production plan changes, authorize emergency supplier relationships, override inventory policies for strategic initiatives
CEO (Level 6 - Ultimate Authority)

Authority Scope: Final authority on all matters, resolve conflicts between CFO and COO, authorize extraordinary actions with board notification
Cannot: Delete audit logs, disable governance framework
Examples: Approve transactions requiring board visibility, authorize emergency protocol activation, resolve deadlocked approvals
SYSTEM (Automated Enforcement)

Authority Scope: Enforce non-discretionary rules, trigger automatic escalations, execute timeout decisions, log all governance events
Cannot: Override explicit human decisions, suppress governance violations
Examples: Auto-escalate expired approvals, auto-reject out-of-policy transactions, trigger alerts on violation patterns
4.2 Role Assignment Rules
Rule 4.2.1: Users are assigned roles based on organizational position and responsibility, not seniority or tenure.

Rule 4.2.2: Role assignment requires approval from current role holder of equal or higher level plus HR confirmation.

Rule 4.2.3: Role removal is immediate upon termination, resignation, or change in organizational responsibility.

Rule 4.2.4: Emergency role elevation requires documented justification, time limitation, and automatic reversion.

Rule 4.2.5: Role audits conducted quarterly to identify inappropriate assignments (e.g., segregation of duties violations).

4.3 Role Limitations
Limitation 4.3.1: No role may self-approve any governed action. Minimum: initiator and approver must be different individuals.

Limitation 4.3.2: No role may override immutability rules. Corrections via reversal only.

Limitation 4.3.3: No role may delete or modify audit logs. Log access is read-only with separate audit trail.

Limitation 4.3.4: No role may disable governance controls except through formal governance framework modification process (CTO + CFO approval).

Rule 5.4.4: Custom Thresholds

SECTION 6: OVERRIDE POLICY
Overrides are governance exceptions. Every override is a liability event requiring justification, approval, and audit permanence.

6.1 Override Definition
Override: Any action that bypasses normal governance controls, authority requirements, or system-enforced rules.

Override Types:

Type A: Authority Override — Action executed by individual lacking normal authority (e.g., Manager approves transaction normally requiring Director)

Type B: Policy Override — Action that violates established policy rules (e.g., extending credit beyond customer credit limit)

Type C: Control Override — Action that bypasses automated control (e.g., backdating transaction, posting to closed period)

Type D: Threshold Override — Action exceeding normal threshold without proper approval (emergency execution)

6.2 When Overrides Are Permitted
Permitted Override Scenarios:

Emergency Operations: System failure, natural disaster, critical customer situation requiring immediate action outside normal authority

Executive Decision: Strategic decision by CEO/CFO/COO that supersedes normal policy for documented business reason

Regulatory Compliance: Action required by auditor, regulator, or legal authority

System Defect Workaround: Temporary bypass of malfunctioning control pending engineering fix (requires incident ticket)

6.3 Override Requirements (Mandatory)
Requirement 6.3.1: Justification
Every override requires written justification explaining:

Why normal process cannot be followed
Business impact if override is not granted
Alternative considered and rejected
Timeframe for resolution (if temporary)
Requirement 6.3.2: Approval
Overrides require approval from authority level TWO levels above normal requirement.

Normal: Manager approval → Override: Director approval
Normal: Director approval → Override: CFO/COO approval
Normal: CFO approval → Override: CEO approval
Requirement 6.3.3: Time Limitation
All overrides except executive strategic decisions expire after:

Emergency overrides: 24 hours
System defect workarounds: 7 days (renewable with CTO approval)
Policy overrides: Single use (cannot be standing authorization)
Requirement 6.3.4: Documentation
Override approval documented with:

Requesting user and approving authority
Timestamp of request and approval
Justification text (minimum 50 characters)
Business impact assessment
Related transaction references
Requirement 6.3.5: Executive Notification
All overrides notify executive leadership within 1 hour:

CFO notified of all financial overrides
COO notified of all operational overrides
CEO notified of all overrides exceeding $100K or strategic impact
6.4 Prohibited Overrides (Absolute)
The following overrides are NEVER permitted under any circumstances:

Prohibited 6.4.1: Modification of posted journal entries (must use reversal)

Prohibited 6.4.2: Deletion of audit logs

Prohibited 6.4.3: Backdating transactions more than 90 days

Prohibited 6.4.4: Posting transactions to closed fiscal periods (period unlock requires separate process)

Prohibited 6.4.5: Self-approval of overrides (approver must be different from requestor)

Prohibited 6.4.6: Bulk override of multiple transactions (each requires individual approval)

Prohibited 6.4.7: Override of segregation of duties rules

Prohibited 6.4.8: Suppression of governance violation logs

6.5 Override Pattern Detection
Pattern 6.5.1: Frequency Trigger
Same user requesting > 3 overrides in 30 days triggers governance review

Pattern 6.5.2: Approval Concentration
Same approver granting > 10 overrides in 30 days triggers governance review

Pattern 6.5.3: Justification Similarity
Identical or similar justification text used > 2 times triggers governance review (indicates templating, not genuine emergency)

Pattern 6.5.4: Time-of-Day Pattern
Overrides requested outside business hours (after 6pm, weekends) reviewed for fraud indicators

Pattern 6.5.5: Value Clustering
Multiple overrides just below threshold (e.g., multiple $9.9K transactions when threshold is $10K) triggers structuring investigation

6.6 Override Revocation
Revocation 6.6.1: Overrides are revocable. If justification later found invalid, approving authority notified and corrective action required.

Revocation 6.6.2: Abuse of override authority results in role suspension pending investigation.

Revocation 6.6.3: Overrides granted based on false information result in immediate role revocation and disciplinary action.

SECTION 7: ESCALATION RULES
Escalation is automatic and non-discretionary when defined conditions are met.

7.1 Time-Based Escalation
Rule 7.1.1: Approval Timeouts

Purchase Order Approval	24 hours	48 hours → Director	72 hours → CFO/COO	96 hours → Auto-reject + CEO notification
Journal Entry Approval	8 hours	16 hours → Director	24 hours → CFO	48 hours → Auto-reject + Audit Committee notification

Rule 7.1.2: Escalation Notification
At each escalation stage, notifications sent to:

Original approver (notifying of escalation due to inaction)
Escalated approver (requiring action)
Requestor (status update)
Executive dashboard (pattern tracking)
Rule 7.1.3: Business Hours vs. Clock Hours
Timeouts calculated in business hours (Monday-Friday, 8am-6pm local time) unless marked "urgent" (clock hours).

7.2 Value-Based Escalation
Rule 7.2.1: Automatic Threshold Escalation
Actions exceeding thresholds automatically escalate regardless of timeout status.

Rule 7.2.2: Cumulative Value Escalation
Multiple transactions from same user exceeding cumulative threshold trigger escalation:

Daily cumulative: Actions totaling > 5x normal threshold
Weekly cumulative: Actions totaling > 20x normal threshold
Monthly cumulative: Actions totaling > 100x normal threshold
Example: Manager authorized for $10K journal entries. Five entries totaling $60K in one day trigger escalation (exceeds 5x $10K threshold).

7.3 Pattern-Based Escalation
Rule 7.3.1: Repetitive Rejection
Same user's actions rejected > 3 times in 30 days trigger training review and temporary authority suspension.

Rule 7.3.2: Approval Velocity Anomaly
Approver processing > 3 standard deviations above normal approval rate triggers governance review (potential rubber-stamping).

Rule 7.3.3: After-Hours Activity
Material actions (> $50K) executed outside business hours trigger immediate executive notification.

Rule 7.3.4: Geographic Anomaly
Actions initiated from unexpected geographic location trigger security review and temporary hold.

7.4 Executive Escalation Rules
Rule 7.4.1: CEO Escalation Triggers

Any action exceeding $1M
Any governance violation classified as "Critical"
Any action requiring board notification
Any deadlocked approval (CFO and COO disagree)
Rule 7.4.2: Board Escalation Triggers

Actions exceeding $5M
Financial restatements
Regulatory violations
Material audit findings
Rule 7.4.3: External Escalation

Suspected fraud → External auditor + Legal
Regulatory inquiry → Compliance officer + Legal + CEO
Cybersecurity incident → CISO + CTO + CEO + Board
7.5 Escalation Documentation
Rule 7.5.1: All escalations logged with:

Original requestor and intended approver
Escalation trigger (timeout, value, pattern)
Escalation recipient
Resolution (approved, rejected, further escalated)
Elapsed time from initiation to resolution
Rule 7.5.2: Escalation analytics reviewed monthly:

Escalation rates by department
Timeout patterns by approver
Rejection rates by requestor
Average time-to-resolution
Rule 7.5.3: Excessive escalation rates (> 10% of transactions) trigger governance review:

Are thresholds too restrictive?
Is approval velocity adequate?
Is training needed?
SECTION 8: IMMUTABILITY RULES
Certain records must be immutable to preserve audit integrity and compliance.

8.1 Financial Immutability
Rule 8.1.1: Posted Journal Entries
Once journal entry is posted (status = "Posted"), it becomes immutable. No field can be modified. Corrections via reversal entry + new entry.

Immutability Scope:

Entry number
Entry date
All line amounts (debit, credit)
Account assignments
Description
Reference numbers
Posting user and timestamp
Correction Process:

Create reversal entry (negative of original)
Reference original entry number
Create new correct entry
Link reversal and new entry to original
Both reversal and new entry require same approval level as original
Rule 8.1.2: Closed Fiscal Periods
Once fiscal period is closed, no transactions can be posted to that period. Period unlock requires CFO approval with documented justification and audit committee notification.

Period Close Scope:

Monthly close: Locked 5 business days after month-end
Quarterly close: Locked 10 business days after quarter-end
Annual close: Locked 20 business days after year-end (after external audit completion)
Unlock Justification Requirements:

Material error discovered (> $10K impact on financial statements)
Regulatory requirement (tax audit adjustment)
External audit adjustment
CEO or CFO written approval
Rule 8.1.3: Bank Reconciliations
Completed reconciliations are immutable. Discrepancies require new reconciliation, not modification of completed one.

8.2 Operational Immutability
Rule 8.2.1: Stock Movements Ledger
All inventory movements are immutable after posting. Corrections via adjustment movements (reversal + new movement).

Immutability Scope:

Movement date and timestamp
Product, location, quantity
Movement type (in, out, transfer, adjustment)
Reference transaction (PO, sales order, work order)
User who posted movement
Correction Process:

Physical count discrepancy: Create "adjustment" movement with supervisor approval
Data entry error: Create reversal movement + correct movement
Both require written justification and photo documentation for physical adjustments
Rule 8.2.2: Completed Work Orders
Work orders in "Completed" or "Closed" status are immutable.

Immutability Scope:

Material consumption records
Labor hours and costs
Produced quantities
Quality inspection results
Completion date
Correction Process:

Material consumption errors: Create adjustment work order
Cost corrections: Create journal entry for cost reclassification
Cannot modify historical production records
Rule 8.2.3: Goods Receipts
Purchase order receipts confirmed and posted are immutable.

Immutability Scope:

Receipt date
Received quantities
Quality inspection results
Received cost
Supplier and PO reference
Correction Process:

Quantity discrepancy: Create return transaction or additional receipt
Cost discrepancy: Create purchase price variance journal entry
Quality issue: Create quality hold + return or scrap transaction
8.3 Human Capital Immutability
Rule 8.3.1: Posted Payroll Runs
Payroll runs in "Posted" status are immutable.

Immutability Scope:

Employee payments (gross, net, deductions)
Employer contributions (BPJS, taxes)
Payroll period
Payment date
GL posting
Correction Process:

Underpayment: Create supplemental payroll run
Overpayment: Create recovery deduction in next payroll
Tax correction: Create adjustment run with tax authority notification
Rule 8.3.2: Employment History
Changes to employment status (hire, promotion, transfer, termination) are immutable once effective date passes.

Immutability Scope:

Hire date
Termination date (if occurred)
Historical salary records
Historical position assignments
Historical performance ratings
Correction Process:

Effective date errors: Create correcting entry with HR Director approval + legal review
Cannot delete employment history (EEOC, labor law compliance)
Rule 8.3.3: Performance Reviews
Finalized performance reviews are immutable after employee acknowledgment or 30 days, whichever is sooner.

Immutability Scope:

Ratings
Comments
Goals assessment
Reviewer and date
Correction Process:

Material error: Create amendment with CHRO approval
Disputed rating: Formal appeal process (does not modify original)
8.4 Immutability Enforcement
Rule 8.4.1: Database-Level Protection
Immutable records protected by database triggers that prevent UPDATE and DELETE operations.

Rule 8.4.2: API-Level Protection
Application APIs reject modification requests for immutable records with error code indicating immutability reason.

Rule 8.4.3: UI-Level Protection
User interfaces do not display edit buttons for immutable records. Display "Immutable" status indicator.

Rule 8.4.4: Emergency Override
Immutability override requires:

CTO approval for system defect only
Written incident documentation
Audit committee notification within 24 hours
External auditor notification if financial data affected
SECTION 9: GOVERNANCE VIOLATION CLASSIFICATION
Violations are categorized by severity. Each category triggers defined system responses.

9.1 Violation Severity Levels
SEVERITY: LOW

Definition: Minor procedural deviation with no financial or operational impact.

Examples:

User attempts action lacking authority (system blocks, no harm)
Approval delayed < 50% beyond normal timeframe
Minor data entry error caught by validation
Informational policy reminder triggered
System Response:

Log violation
Display user warning
No escalation required
Monthly summary report to management
SEVERITY: MEDIUM

Definition: Policy deviation with potential financial or operational impact, or pattern of low-severity violations.

Examples:

Approval timeout (action escalated to next level)
Minor threshold breach (10-25% over limit)
Duplicate transaction attempt (caught by system)
Three low-severity violations by same user in 30 days
Authorization used outside normal business hours
System Response:

Log violation with detailed context
Escalate to supervisor
Weekly summary to director
User receives training reminder
Pattern detection activated
SEVERITY: HIGH

Definition: Material policy violation with confirmed financial or operational impact, or successful execution of unauthorized action.

Examples:

Material threshold breach (> 25% over limit)
Successful execution of action lacking proper authority (control failure)
Override granted without proper justification
Backdated transaction > 30 days
Attempted modification of immutable record
Five medium-severity violations by same user in 30 days
System Response:

Log violation as high-priority incident
Immediate notification to director and executive
User authority suspended pending review
Incident investigation required within 24 hours
Corrective action plan required
External audit notification if financial impact > $10K
SEVERITY: CRITICAL

Definition: Severe governance breach indicating fraud, system compromise, or regulatory violation.

Examples:

Modification of posted journal entries (immutability breach)
Deletion or modification of audit logs
Backdated transaction > 90 days
Successful override of segregation of duties
Pattern indicating fraud (structuring, collusion)
Unauthorized access to PII or financial data
System configuration change bypassing governance
System Response:

Log as critical security incident
Immediate notification to CEO, CFO, CTO
User account suspended immediately (no delay)
All user actions frozen pending investigation
Formal investigation initiated (internal audit + legal)
External auditor notification within 24 hours
Law enforcement notification if fraud suspected
Board notification within 48 hours
9.2 Violation Handling Procedures
Procedure 9.2.1: Immediate Actions (Within 1 Hour)

Log violation with complete context
Execute automatic system response per severity level
Notify required parties per escalation matrix
Freeze related transactions if applicable
Preserve all evidence (logs, screenshots, documents)
Procedure 9.2.2: Investigation (Within 24 Hours for High/Critical)

Assign investigation owner (internal audit or security)
Interview involved parties
Review related transactions and patterns
Determine root cause (user error, training gap, control deficiency, fraud)
Assess financial and operational impact
Document findings
Procedure 9.2.3: Resolution (Timeframe per Severity)

Low: Resolution within 7 days (typically training or process clarification)
Medium: Resolution within 3 days (may include control adjustment)
High: Resolution within 24 hours (may include disciplinary action)
Critical: Immediate containment, formal resolution within 48 hours
Procedure 9.2.4: Corrective Action

User training required for user error violations
Control adjustment for systematic control deficiencies
Disciplinary action for willful policy violations
Termination and legal action for fraud
System enhancement for technical vulnerabilities
Procedure 9.2.5: Post-Violation Monitoring

User with high/critical violation: Enhanced monitoring for 90 days
Control that failed: Weekly audit for 30 days
Systemic issue: Cross-department audit to identify other occurrences
9.3 Repeat Violation Handling
Rule 9.3.1: Violation Escalation
Repeated violations by same user escalate severity automatically:

3 Low → 1 Medium
3 Medium → 1 High
2 High → 1 Critical + formal investigation
Rule 9.3.2: Progressive Discipline

First medium violation: Written warning + mandatory training
Second medium violation: Probation + role restriction
Third medium violation: Role suspension pending review
First high violation: Immediate suspension + investigation
First critical violation: Termination + legal review
Rule 9.3.3: Reinstatement Requirements
After suspension for governance violation:

Completion of mandatory governance training
Written acknowledgment of policy understanding
Probationary period (30-90 days) with enhanced monitoring
Executive approval for reinstatement
9.4 Violation Reporting
Rule 9.4.1: Real-Time Dashboard
Executive dashboard displays:

All high and critical violations (real-time)
Medium violations (daily summary)
Low violations (weekly trend)
Violation rates by department
Repeat violators
Rule 9.4.2: Regular Reporting

Weekly: High/Critical violation summary to executives
Monthly: All violations summary to audit committee
Quarterly: Governance effectiveness report to board
Rule 9.4.3: External Reporting

Annual: Auditor access to complete violation log
As required: Regulatory agency inquiries
Material events: Board notification within 48 hours

SECTION 11: ENFORCEMENT STATEMENT
11.1 Absolute Enforcement
This governance framework is non-negotiable and binding on all system components, users, customizations, and integrations.

Enforcement 11.1.1: No product feature may bypass governance controls.

Enforcement 11.1.2: No customer customization may disable or circumvent governance rules.

Enforcement 11.1.3: No integration (internal or external) may write data without governance validation.

Enforcement 11.1.4: No user, regardless of role or seniority, is exempt from governance controls.

Enforcement 11.1.5: No operational urgency justifies permanent governance bypass. Emergency protocols exist for time-critical situations, but they increase (not decrease) audit scrutiny.

11.2 Technical Enforcement
Enforcement 11.2.1: Database Layer
Governance rules enforced by database triggers and constraints. Application layer bypass is prevented.

Enforcement 11.2.2: API Layer
All API endpoints validate governance requirements before execution. No "trusted" APIs exempt from validation.

Enforcement 11.2.3: UI Layer
User interfaces prevent initiation of unauthorized actions. Users don't see options they cannot execute.

Enforcement 11.2.4: Integration Layer
External system integrations subject to same governance as internal users. API keys have role assignments and authority limits.

11.3 Organizational Enforcement
Enforcement 11.3.1: Engineering
Engineers cannot deploy code that circumvents governance. Code reviews include governance compliance check.

Enforcement 11.3.2: Product
Product managers cannot approve features that weaken governance. Feature proposals include governance impact assessment.

Enforcement 11.3.3: Sales
Sales cannot promise customizations that bypass governance. Customer contracts include governance compliance clause.

Enforcement 11.3.4: Implementation
Consultants cannot configure system to disable governance. Implementation checklists include governance validation.

11.4 Consequence of Non-Compliance
Consequence 11.4.1: System Level
Any system component found to bypass governance is immediately disabled pending fix. No exceptions.

Consequence 11.4.2: User Level
Users attempting to bypass governance (not just violate rules, but attempt to circumvent the framework) face immediate suspension and investigation.

Consequence 11.4.3: Vendor Level
Third-party integrations violating governance have API access revoked immediately.

Consequence 11.4.4: Organizational Level
Departments with pattern of governance violations face enhanced monitoring and potential leadership changes.

11.5 Governance Framework Modification
Rule 11.5.1: Modification Authority
Changes to this governance framework require joint approval from CTO and CFO with documented business justification and risk assessment.

Rule 11.5.2: Material Changes
Material changes (threshold modifications, role changes, immutability rule changes) require CEO approval and audit committee notification.

Rule 11.5.3: Emergency Changes
No emergency justifies governance framework suspension. Emergency protocols work within the framework, not outside it.

Rule 11.5.4: Customer-Specific Changes
Customer-specific governance modifications are permissible only if they increase (not decrease) control rigor. Customers cannot purchase "governance waivers."
